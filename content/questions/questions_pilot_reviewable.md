## Q-0001 — Anemia in Oncology Patients  `C` · moderate
*1. Clinical Practice* · node `S1.c` · support: moderate

A 58-year-old woman with multiple myeloma is midway through a cycle of myelosuppressive chemotherapy. Over several weeks her hemoglobin has declined gradually from 11.2 g/dL to 6.8 g/dL. On evaluation she is afebrile with normal vital signs, ambulates without dyspnea, and has no evidence of active bleeding or cardiac ischemia. As part of a concurrent blood utilization review, the transfusion medicine service is asked why she has remained clinically stable despite this hemoglobin level and whether an RBC transfusion is indicated.

Which of the following best explains her tolerance of this hemoglobin level and is most consistent with evidence-based blood utilization review criteria for a stable patient?

- **A.** Gradual, chronic anemia allows compensatory mechanisms—increased cardiac output, increased RBC 2,3-DPG causing a rightward shift of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve, and redistribution of blood flow to vital organs—to maintain tissue oxygen delivery; review criteria for stable patients typically use a hemoglobin threshold around 7 g/dL rather than transfusing by number alone ✅
- **B.** Chemotherapy has decreased her RBC 2,3-DPG, shifting the oxyhemoglobin curve to the left and increasing hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen, which improves oxygen delivery to her tissues
- **C.** Because her hemoglobin is below 7 g/dL, prospective review criteria mandate immediate transfusion regardless of her clinical status
- **D.** Transfusing RBC units now would immediately normalize tissue oxygen delivery because stored red cells have elevated 2,3-DPG levels compared to native red cells
- **E.** This scenario should instead be evaluated by retrospective review, since retrospective review is the appropriate model for real-time transfusion decisions

**Correct (A):** In gradually developing (chronic) anemia, the body has time to activate compensatory physiologic mechanisms: increased cardiac output, increased RBC 2,3-DPG (which shifts the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve to the right and facilitates oxygen release to tissues), and redistribution of blood flow to vital organs. These adaptations allow a hemodynamically stable, asymptomatic patient to tolerate a hemoglobin level that would be poorly tolerated if it fell acutely. This physiology underlies evidence-based blood utilization review criteria, which use thresholds such as hemoglobin <7 g/dL for stable patients as a guideline for evaluating transfusion appropriateness rather than transfusing reflexively by number.
> Pearl: Gradual, chronic anemia (as commonly seen with myelosuppressive chemotherapy in oncology patients) triggers physiologic compensation—increased cardiac output, a 2,3-DPG-driven rightward shift of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve enhancing tissue O2 release, and blood flow redistribution—that can preserve adequate oxygenation despite a low hemoglobin. Evidence-based blood utilization review uses hemoglobin thresholds (e.g., <7 g/dL in stable patients) as guidance for evaluating transfusion appropriateness, not as an automatic transfusion trigger; clinicians must also recall that stored RBC units carry a storage-lesion-related decrease (not increase) in 2,3-DPG.

- *B wrong:* Incorrect and directionally reversed: decreased 2,3-DPG causes a LEFT shift, which increases oxygen affinity and impairs (not improves) oxygen release to tissues. This is the opposite of the physiologic right-shift compensation that actually occurs in chronic anemia.
- *C wrong:* Incorrect: a hemoglobin threshold used in blood utilization review criteria (e.g., <7 g/dL for stable patients) is a guideline for evaluating whether transfusion is justified, not an automatic mandate that overrides clinical assessment of a stable, asymptomatic patient.
- *D wrong:* Incorrect and factually backward: stored RBC units have DECREASED 2,3-DPG (a recognized storage lesion), which causes a left shift and can transiently impair, not improve, oxygen unloading after transfusion.
- *E wrong:* Incorrect: retrospective review occurs days to weeks after transfusion and is best suited for large-scale trending and benchmarking, not real-time decisions. Prospective (pre-issue) or concurrent (within 12-24 hours) review models are used for timely transfusion decisions like this one.

- *E wrong:* Incorrect distractor. Autologous HPC/blood products are not exempt from infectious disease testing; testing is still required, for example when the product is shipped to another facility.

## Q-0075 — Current Good Manufacturing Practice  `C` · moderate

The lapse was discovered later the same shift during a routine review of QC logs. The affected unit had not yet been released from the blood bank; it was immediately quarantined, the reagent lot was pulled from use, and the unit was destroyed. The unit was never issued to any patient or shipped to another facility. The quality manager documents the event, performs a root cause analysis, and implements a corrective action.

- **A.** Reagent quality control lapses fall outside the scope of cGMP oversight, so they never qualify as manufacturing deviations.
- **B.** The affected unit was never distributed — it remained within the facility's control and was destroyed before release — so the criterion requiring an already-distributed product was not met. ✅
- **C.** The event does not require reporting because it was discovered and corrected within 45 days, the FDA's allowed window for resolving deviations without notification.

- *D wrong:* Incorrect. - *E wrong:* Incorrect. A near-miss or nonconforming event is not exempt from documentation or investigation — it must still be captured and investigated under the QMS; the reason no BPD report is owed here is specifically the unmet distribution criterion, not a blanket exemption for near-misses.
