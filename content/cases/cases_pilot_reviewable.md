## C-001 — Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction from ABO-Incompatible RBC Transfusion Due to Clerical Error  `C`
*6. Hazards of Transfusion: Specific Adverse Events* · support: high

**Setup.** A 54-year-old man with an upper GI bleed is started on his first unit of crossmatched RBCs on a general medicine floor. The unit was retrieved by a nurse covering for a colleague during a busy shift change; the bedside identification check was performed quickly. About 10 minutes into the transfusion, the patient becomes acutely uncomfortable, reporting chills, severe flank and low back pain, and a sense of "impending doom." He is visibly diaphoretic and anxious. The nurse notes his blood pressure has dropped and his Foley bag now contains dark, tea-colored urine that was clear an hour earlier.

**Findings.**
- Temperature: 38.6°C, risen from a pre-transfusion baseline of 37.0°C, with associated rigors (abnormal)
- Heart rate: 128 bpm (baseline ~90 bpm) (abnormal)
- Blood pressure: 82/48 mmHg (baseline 128/76 mmHg) (critical)
- Reported symptoms: Flank/back pain, chills, sense of impending doom (abnormal)
- Urine appearance (Foley): Dark red/tea-colored, new since transfusion start (abnormal)
- Post-reaction plasma inspection: Visibly pink/red discoloration consistent with hemoglobinemia (abnormal)
- Direct antiglobulin test (DAT), post-transfusion sample: Positive (abnormal)
- Repeat ABO/Rh grouping (patient vs. implicated unit): Patient types as group O; the transfused unit retypes as group A — ABO discrepancy identified, confirming the wrong unit was administered (critical)
- Haptoglobin: Decreased (abnormal)
- LDH: Elevated (abnormal)
- Indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin: Elevated (abnormal)
- Peripheral blood smear: Spherocytes present (abnormal)
- Urinalysis: Hemoglobinuria (dipstick blood-positive with few/no intact RBCs on microscopy) (abnormal)

**Decision points.**
1. Ten minutes into the transfusion the patient develops fever, chills, flank pain, and hypotension. What is the single most important immediate bedside action?
   - **Answer:** Stop the transfusion immediately, maintain IV access with 0.9% saline, perform an immediate clerical recheck at the bedside, and notify the physician and blood bank.
   - This constellation — fever, chills, flank/back pain, hypotension, and a feeling of impending doom — is the classic presentation of an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. The mandated initial bedside action for any suspected transfusion reaction of this severity is to stop the transfusion, keep venous access open with normal saline, perform a clerical recheck, and notify the physician and blood bank immediately (SRC-045).
2. After stopping the transfusion, what diagnostic workup should be sent to confirm or refute a hemolytic transfusion reaction, and what is the leading root cause to investigate?
   - **Answer:** Visually inspect the post-reaction plasma for hemoglobinemia, send a post-transfusion DAT, and repeat ABO/Rh grouping on both the patient's sample and the implicated unit as part of a full clerical recheck. Clerical/identification error ("wrong blood in tube"/patient misidentification) is the leading, often entirely preventable, cause of acute intravascular hemolytic reactions and must be actively investigated.
   - The standard lab workup for a suspected hemolytic reaction is visual inspection of plasma for hemoglobinemia, a DAT on the post-transfusion sample, and repeat ABO grouping (SRC-045). Clerical errors remain the primary cause of acute intravascular hemolytic reactions, which is why barcode/electronic ID systems exist to reduce wrong-blood-in-tube events (SRC-032; SRC-045).
3. Results return: pink plasma, positive DAT, decreased haptoglobin, elevated LDH and indirect bilirubin, spherocytes, and hemoglobinuria, with an ABO discrepancy confirming a group A unit was given to a group O patient. Which category and mechanism of hemolysis does this represent, and how does it differ from a delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR)?
   - **Answer:** This is an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR): intravascular, complement-mediated hemolysis, classically triggered by IgM anti-A/anti-B in ABO-incompatible transfusion, occurring during or within 24 hours of transfusion. DHTR, by contrast, is typically extravascular and IgG-mediated (an anamnestic alloantibody response), presenting with a positive DAT and a newly identified alloantibody or positive eluate 24 hours to 28 days after transfusion.
   - AHTR is usually intravascular (IgM/complement) and immediate, evidenced here by hemoglobinuria, decreased haptoglobin, elevated LDH/bilirubin, and spherocytes; DHTR is usually extravascular (IgG) and occurs days later with a positive DAT and newly identified alloantibody or eluate (SRC-045). This alloimmune, ABO-specific pattern also differs from an autoimmune process: in warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia the eluate is characteristically panreactive against essentially all cells tested, whereas an alloantibody-mediated reaction like this one reflects a specific ABO incompatibility rather than a panreactive autoantibody (SRC-061).
4. The patient remains hypotensive with declining urine output. What are the priority management steps, and can this transfusion episode be resumed once he stabilizes?
   - **Answer:** Give vigorous IV fluid resuscitation targeting urine output >1 mL/kg/hr, treat shock and DIC if they develop, and do not resume the implicated unit under any circumstances. Report the event through institutional hemovigilance as a Definitive-imputability, severe (or life-threatening) reaction; a fatal outcome would require FDA notification.
   - AHTR management centers on vigorous fluid resuscitation to maintain urine output above 1 mL/kg/hr and treatment of shock and DIC (SRC-045). Resuming the unit is a classic board trap: transfusion may only be restarted after successful treatment of an isolated, simple urticarial reaction with no other symptoms — it is never appropriate for fever, chills, pain, or any hemolytic reaction (SRC-045). Under NHSN hemovigilance criteria this event would be graded Definitive imputability (fulfills case-definition criteria) and Severe/Life-threatening based on clinical course, and any transfusion-related fatality must be reported to the FDA (CBER) as soon as possible with a written report to follow within 7 days (SRC-032).

**Key pathway.** Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction (AHTR) due to ABO incompatibility from a clerical ("wrong blood in tube"/patient-misidentification) error. Correct pathway: (1) STOP the transfusion immediately, keep the IV open with 0.9% saline, perform an immediate bedside clerical recheck, and notify the physician and blood bank; (2) send a post-reaction sample for visual inspection of plasma (hemoglobinemia), a post-transfusion DAT, and a repeat ABO/Rh type-and-recheck on both patient and implicated unit; (3) recognize the resulting pattern — positive DAT, pink/red plasma, decreased haptoglobin, elevated LDH and indirect bilirubin, spherocytes, and hemoglobinuria — as intravascular, complement-mediated hemolysis typical of AHTR (as opposed to the extravascular, IgG-mediated, delayed pattern of DHTR); (4) treat with vigorous fluid resuscitation targeting urine output >1 mL/kg/hr, manage shock/DIC as needed, permanently discontinue (do NOT resume) the implicated unit, and report the event through institutional hemovigilance with a Definitive imputability/severe-to-life-threatening grading, escalating to FDA notification if the outcome were fatal.

**Teaching.** Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR) is accelerated RBC destruction occurring during or within 24 hours of transfusion, and clerical/patient-identification error is its leading, largely preventable cause — which is precisely why barcode and electronic identification systems exist. The classic presentation (fever, chills, flank/back pain, hypotension, and a sense of impending doom) reflects complement activation and intravascular hemolysis, most often driven by IgM anti-A/anti-B in ABO-incompatible transfusion. The confirmatory laboratory pattern is hemoglobinemia (pink/red plasma) and hemoglobinuria, decreased haptoglobin, elevated LDH and indirect bilirubin, spherocytes on smear, and a positive post-transfusion DAT with a repeat ABO discrepancy pinpointing the wrong-unit error. This intravascular/IgM/complement/immediate picture is the key contrast point against delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR), which is extravascular, IgG-mediated (an anamnestic response to a previously formed alloantibody), and presents 24 hours to 28 days later with a positive DAT and a newly identified alloantibody or eluate. Management is supportive and urgent: stop the transfusion, keep the line open with saline, perform an immediate clerical recheck, notify the blood bank/physician, and then aggressively resuscitate with fluids to protect renal perfusion (urine output goal >1 mL/kg/hr) while monitoring for and treating shock or DIC. Unlike an isolated urticarial reaction — the only reaction type in which the same unit may be resumed after treatment — a hemolytic reaction always mandates permanent discontinuation of that unit. Once identified, the event is graded through hemovigilance frameworks (NHSN imputability and severity criteria) and, if fatal, must be reported to the FDA within defined timelines. Learners should also keep two related concepts in mind: hemolysis with a negative DAT points away from immune hemolysis toward nonimmune mechanical or thermal causes (small-bore needles, pumps, blood warmers, or inappropriate IV fluids like D5W), and in sickle cell disease patients a severe post-transfusion hemolytic event raises concern for hyperhemolysis syndrome, in which both donor and autologous RBCs are destroyed and further transfusion must be avoided.

**Pitfalls.**
- Assuming isolated fever/chills during a transfusion is simply a febrile nonhemolytic transfusion reaction (FNHTR) without first excluding hemolysis via DAT, haptoglobin, LDH, and plasma inspection — AHTR must always be actively ruled out when fever/chills occur during transfusion.
- Believing the transfusion can be restarted once the patient stabilizes: resuming the unit is only acceptable after successful treatment of an isolated, simple urticarial reaction with no other symptoms — never for fever, chills, pain, or any hemolytic process.
- Forgetting that hemolysis with a negative DAT is not necessarily AHTR — nonimmune causes such as mechanical damage (small needles, pumps), improper temperature control (blood warmers), or infusion with an unapproved IV solution (e.g., D5W) can also produce hemolysis without an immune mechanism.
- In a sickle cell disease patient with a severe post-transfusion hemolytic event, reflexively transfusing more RBCs to treat resulting anemia without considering hyperhemolysis syndrome, in which both donor and autologous RBCs are destroyed and further transfusion must be withheld.

## C-002 — Fever, Flank Pain, and "Impending Doom" During Transfusion: Recognizing Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction  `C`
*6. Hazards of Transfusion: Specific Adverse Events* · support: high

**Setup.** A 58-year-old man is being transfused one unit of RBCs for symptomatic anemia. Approximately 10 minutes into the infusion he develops rigors, low-back/flank pain, and tells the nurse he feels a sense of "impending doom." His temperature rises to 39.4°C, heart rate climbs to 128 bpm, and his urine turns red-brown over the next hour. The transfusion is stopped and a clerical/laboratory workup is initiated.

**Findings.**
- Temperature: 39.4°C, onset ~10 minutes into transfusion of the RBC unit (critical)
- Heart rate: 128 bpm (tachycardia; source-defined threshold >100 bpm) (abnormal)
- Patient-reported symptoms: Rigors/chills, low-back and flank pain, and a self-described sense of "impending doom" (critical)
- Urine appearance: Red-brown urine, dipstick heme-positive without intact RBCs on microscopy — hemoglobinuria (abnormal)
- Renal function: Rising creatinine with decreasing urine output over subsequent hours (evolving renal failure) (critical)
- Repeat ABO/Rh typing (clerical recheck): Archived pretransfusion patient sample types as Group O; the issued/transfused unit types as Group A — mismatch identified (critical)
- Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT), post-reaction EDTA sample: Positive with polyspecific AHG reagent, indicating in-vivo RBC coating with IgG and/or complement (C3d) (abnormal)
- Post-transfusion hemoglobin increment: +0.2 g/dL after 1 RBC unit, versus the ~1 g/dL rise expected per unit in an average adult (abnormal)

**Decision points.**
1. Ten minutes into the transfusion the patient develops fever, rigors, flank pain, a sense of "impending doom," and red-brown urine. What is the single most important immediate action?
   - **Answer:** Stop the transfusion immediately, maintain IV access with saline, and initiate a clerical/identity check while notifying the physician and blood bank.
   - This constellation — fever, chills, hemoglobinuria, and a feeling of "impending doom," with risk of renal failure — is the classic presentation of acute intravascular hemolysis from ABO incompatibility (SRC-024). Because clerical errors (patient/sample/unit misidentification) remain the primary, largely preventable cause of acute intravascular hemolytic reactions (SRC-032), stopping the transfusion and verifying identity is both the safety-critical first step and the most efficient diagnostic step.
2. The bedside wristband matches, but a mismatch was found between the patient's archived pretransfusion type and the issued unit. What should be sent to the blood bank to confirm the reaction and its cause?
   - **Answer:** Return the unit/tubing plus a fresh EDTA post-transfusion sample for repeat ABO/Rh typing, DAT, and antibody screen/identification.
   - Forward and reverse ABO/Rh typing is mandatory for confirming identity/compatibility problems (SRC-024), and the antibody screen/panel (using reagent O-cells) identifies any unexpected clinically significant antibody (SRC-024). The DAT detects in-vivo coating of RBCs with IgG or complement (SRC-032); EDTA is the preferred specimen for DAT because clotted specimens can show in-vitro complement attachment and yield a false positive (SRC-032). Any negative AHG/DAT result is only interpretable if IgG-coated check cells were added and reacted, confirming the reagent and technique were valid (SRC-032).
3. The patient's post-transfusion hemoglobin rose only 0.2 g/dL (well below the ~1 g/dL expected per unit) and he required escalated inpatient care overnight but survived. How should this event be classified and handled from a hemovigilance standpoint?
   - **Answer:** Classify by NHSN severity (severe/life-threatening) and imputability criteria, document/report through hemovigilance, and apply FDA/CBER death-reporting timelines only if the reaction proves fatal.
   - NHSN severity grading defines Severe as requiring/prolonging inpatient hospitalization and Life-threatening as immediate risk of death (SRC-032); imputability is graded Definitive/Probable/Possible/Doubtful based on how well the case meets criteria (SRC-032). Hemolytic reactions are among the transfusion reactions most frequently reported to the FDA as fatal (SRC-032), and suspected transfusion-related deaths specifically require prompt oral/electronic FDA notification followed by a written report within 7 days (SRC-032). The blunted hemoglobin increment (well under the expected ~1 g/dL per unit, per SRC-024/SRC-032) is itself objective evidence that the transfused cells were being destroyed, supporting the severity assessment.

**Key pathway.** The vignette is a clerical-error ABO-incompatible acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR). Correct pathway: (1) Recognize the classic acute intravascular hemolysis symptom complex — fever, chills, flank/low-back pain, "impending doom," and hemoglobinuria — occurring during transfusion, and immediately STOP the transfusion, keep the IV line open with saline, and begin a bedside clerical/identity check, since clerical error (misidentification) is the leading preventable cause of this reaction. (2) Confirm and characterize the reaction by returning the unit/tubing plus a fresh EDTA post-transfusion patient sample to the blood bank for repeat ABO/Rh forward-and-reverse typing (pre- vs post-transfusion), a DAT to detect in-vivo IgG/complement coating, and an antibody screen/panel — while remembering that a negative AHG result is only valid if IgG-coated check cells reacted. (3) Grade the event using NHSN severity (non-severe/severe/life-threatening/death) and imputability (definitive/probable/possible/doubtful) criteria, document it through the hemovigilance/nonconformance system, and, if the reaction had been fatal, notify FDA/CBER as soon as possible with a written follow-up within 7 days — recognizing that hemolytic reactions remain among the most frequently reported fatal transfusion complications.

**Teaching.** Hemolytic transfusion reactions are one of the leading causes of transfusion-related fatality reported to the FDA, alongside TRALI and TACO (SRC-032). The classic acute, intravascular presentation — most often from ABO incompatibility — produces fever, chills, hemoglobinuria, renal failure, and a feeling of "impending doom" (SRC-024), and the single most common preventable root cause is a clerical error in patient, sample, or unit identification rather than an exotic antibody (SRC-032). Recognizing this symptom cluster during transfusion should trigger an immediate stop, maintenance of IV access, and a bedside identity check before any further laboratory workup. Confirmatory testing leans on core immunohematology tools: repeat forward/reverse ABO-Rh typing to catch mislabeling (SRC-024), a DAT to demonstrate in-vivo IgG/complement coating of red cells (SRC-032), and an antibody screen/panel to identify a causative alloantibody (SRC-024). Two technical pitfalls matter here: DAT specimens should be EDTA rather than clotted serum, since clotted samples can develop in-vitro complement attachment and falsely suggest hemolysis (SRC-032); and any negative AHG-based test (DAT or antibody screen) is only valid once IgG-coated check cells have been shown to react (SRC-032). Once a reaction is confirmed, it is formally graded through the NHSN hemovigilance framework — severity (non-severe/severe/life-threatening/death) and imputability (definitive/probable/possible/doubtful) — which drives both internal quality management and external FDA reporting obligations for fatal cases (SRC-032). Boards also test whether learners understand that not every DAT-positive, hemolysis-appearing picture is an alloimmune transfusion reaction: autoimmune hemolytic anemia also classically presents with a positive DAT (SRC-027), and thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura produces its own pentad of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, neurologic signs, fever, and renal dysfunction that can superficially overlap with a hemolytic picture (SRC-023) — both are important differentials to keep in mind before anchoring on a transfusion reaction. Finally, the "dosage" phenomenon — antibodies (classically in the Rh, Duffy, Kidd, and MNS systems) reacting more strongly against homozygous-antigen red cells — helps explain why some hemolytic transfusion reactions do not present with the dramatic acute triad at all, and instead surface later as an unexplained failure to achieve the expected hemoglobin increment (SRC-024).

**Pitfalls.**
- Jumping to a rare-antibody workup before completing the clerical/identity check: clerical error (patient, sample, or unit misidentification) is the leading preventable cause of acute intravascular (ABO-incompatible) hemolytic transfusion reactions, so identity verification is a required early step, not an afterthought (SRC-032).
- Sending a clotted (serum) sample for the DAT instead of EDTA: clotted specimens can develop in-vitro complement attachment and produce a false-positive DAT, so specimen type affects interpretation of hemolysis workups (SRC-032).
- Accepting a negative AHG-based result (DAT or antibody screen) without confirming that IgG-coated check cells reacted — a negative result is invalid if the check cells fail to react, since this is the only way to confirm the AHG reagent was added and functional (SRC-032).
- Assuming all hemolytic transfusion reactions present with the dramatic acute triad of fever/chills/hemoglobinuria/"impending doom": antibodies exhibiting dosage (e.g., Kidd, Duffy, Rh, MNS) can be weak or undetectable on a pretransfusion screen and instead present later simply as a blunted-than-expected hemoglobin increment (SRC-024).

## C-003 — Differential Diagnosis of Hemolytic Transfusion Reactions: AHTR vs DHTR vs Non-Hemolytic Mimickers  `C`
*6. Hazards of Transfusion: Specific Adverse Events* · support: moderate

**Setup.** A 39-year-old woman with sickle cell disease, transfused on several prior admissions, is about 30 minutes into a red blood cell transfusion given for symptomatic anemia when she develops fever and hypotension. She is stabilized and the reaction is investigated. Later, after receiving a properly ABO/Rh-matched, crossmatch-compatible unit during a subsequent admission, she returns 8 days afterward with fatigue and an unexplained falling hemoglobin, without fever or hypotension.

**Findings.**
- Onset relative to transfusion start (Stage 1): ~30 minutes into the unit
- Temperature (Stage 1): 38.1°C, up from a pre-transfusion baseline of 37.0°C (rise <2°C) (abnormal)
- Blood pressure (Stage 1): 84/52 mmHg (baseline 118/74 mmHg) (critical)
- Oxygen saturation, room air (Stage 1): 96% (normal)
- PaO2/FiO2 ratio (Stage 1): >300 (no hypoxemia) (normal)
- Jugular venous distension / S3 gallop (Stage 1): Absent (normal)
- Chest radiograph (Stage 1): No bilateral infiltrates (normal)
- Repeat ABO/Rh typing, post-reaction sample (Stage 2): Patient types as group O; transfused unit was labeled group A (critical)
- Direct antiglobulin test (DAT), post-reaction sample (Stage 2): Newly positive (abnormal)
- Patient and unit blood cultures, sent to evaluate for bacterial contamination (Stage 2): Pending/no growth at initial follow-up
- Hemoglobin, day 8 after subsequent (correctly matched) transfusion (Stage 3): 8.1 g/dL, down from a satisfactory post-transfusion value of 10.6 g/dL (adult reference range 12.0-16.0 g/dL) (abnormal)
- Antibody screen, immediately pretransfusion (historical) (Stage 3): Negative (normal)
- Antibody screen, day 8 (Stage 3): Newly positive (abnormal)
- DAT, day 8 (Stage 3): Positive (abnormal)
- Eluate reactivity, day 8 (Stage 3): Reactive with Jk(a+) panel cells only; nonreactive with Jk(a-) cells and with patient's own (autologous) cells (abnormal)
- Fever/hypotension at day-8 presentation (Stage 3): Absent; vital signs stable (normal)

**Decision points.**
1. Thirty minutes into an RBC transfusion, this patient develops fever and hypotension. Before this is treated as a straightforward hemolytic transfusion reaction, which competing diagnoses must be actively excluded, and what findings argue against each?
   - **Answer:** TACO and TRALI are excluded on this presentation; TAS cannot be excluded and requires culture confirmation, while AHTR is pursued in parallel via clerical and serologic workup.
   - TACO is characterized by hypertension, tachycardia, JVD, an S3 gallop, and a BNP/NT-proBNP rise of at least 1.5x baseline; this patient is hypotensive with no JVD/S3, arguing against TACO. TRALI requires severe hypoxemia (SpO2 <90%, PaO2/FiO2 <=300) and bilateral noncardiogenic infiltrates; her SpO2, PaO2/FiO2, and chest film are normal, arguing against TRALI. Bacterial contamination (TAS) classically causes a fever rise of >2°C with rigors and profound hypotension and, being a diagnosis that depends partly on culture results, cannot be excluded from the bedside picture alone even though her fever rise here is milder than that threshold; patient and unit cultures must still be sent.
2. The post-reaction sample shows the patient typing as group O, but the transfused unit was labeled group A, and the post-reaction DAT is newly positive. What is the diagnosis, and what confirms it?
   - **Answer:** Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR) due to ABO-incompatible transfusion from a bedside/identification (clerical) error.
   - AHTR is the immune, acute (<24 hour) reaction classified as ABO-mediated. The discrepancy between the patient's confirmed group-O type and the group-A labeled unit demonstrates a clerical/identification error, and the newly positive DAT reflects immune-mediated destruction of the incompatible transfused cells. This DAT-centered confirmation mirrors the general immunohematologic approach to transfusion-reaction workup, where DAT and downstream elution/antibody identification are used to establish the cause of hemolysis.
3. After recovering and later receiving a properly ABO/Rh-matched, crossmatch-compatible unit, the patient returns 8 days later with fatigue and a falling hemoglobin, without fever or hypotension. Her antibody screen, negative immediately pretransfusion, is now positive, and her DAT is positive. How do you differentiate a delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction from a warm autoimmune process here, and which antibody system is classically implicated?
   - **Answer:** Delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR) from an anamnestic secondary immune response, classically implicated with Kidd (Jka) antibodies.
   - A newly positive antibody screen or DAT appearing after transfusion indicates a secondary (anamnestic) immune response, which is the defining serologic feature of DHTR. Kidd system antibodies are specifically associated with rapid titer decline between exposures and with delayed hemolytic transfusion reactions, explaining why the immediate pretransfusion screen was negative despite presumed prior sensitization. The eluate reacting specifically with Jk(a+) cells and not with the patient's own autologous cells argues against the panreactive pattern that is the hallmark eluate finding in warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia, so this cell-specific reactivity is what distinguishes an alloantibody-driven DHTR from an autoimmune process.

**Key pathway.** Stage 1 (intra-transfusion fever + hypotension): TACO is excluded by the absence of hypertension, JVD/S3, and BNP rise; TRALI is excluded by normal SpO2, normal PaO2/FiO2, and a clear chest film; TAS cannot be excluded on presentation alone (fever rise is <2°C here, which argues against it, but patient/unit cultures must still be sent) — AHTR and TAS remain the leading considerations pending clerical and serologic workup. Stage 2 (clerical + serologic confirmation): a repeat-sample ABO discrepancy (patient O, unit labeled A) plus a newly positive post-reaction DAT confirms an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction (AHTR) from ABO incompatibility due to a bedside/identification (clerical) error — stop the transfusion, verify identification, and proceed with the standard immunohematologic workup. Stage 3 (delayed presentation after a correctly matched unit): an unexplained hemoglobin drop 8 days later, with a newly positive antibody screen/DAT (negative pretransfusion) and an eluate reactive specifically with Jk(a+) cells (not panreactive/not reactive with autologous cells), confirms a delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR) from an anamnestic anti-Jka response rather than a warm autoimmune hemolytic process.

**Teaching.** Differential diagnosis of hemolytic transfusion reactions is built on timing, hemodynamics, and serology working together. Acutely, non-hemolytic mimickers are excluded first: TACO by its hypertensive, volume-overloaded picture with JVD/S3 and a rising BNP, and TRALI by its hypoxemic picture with bilateral noncardiogenic infiltrates; bacterial contamination (TAS) is a fever-and-hypotension mimicker that cannot be excluded without cultures, since its hallmark is an even larger fever spike with rigors and profound hypotension. Once these are excluded or pending, AHTR is confirmed the same way any unexplained incompatibility or hemolytic picture is worked up: a clerical/ABO discrepancy plus a newly positive DAT. Delayed reactions look different by design — they surface more than 24 hours out, are driven by anamnestic alloantibody responses (classically Kidd, which evanesces below detectable levels between exposures), and are confirmed serologically by a newly positive screen/DAT together with an eluate that shows specific, not panreactive, reactivity. That same panreactive-versus-specific eluate distinction is also how a true DHTR is separated from a warm autoimmune hemolytic process uncovered around the time of transfusion.

**Pitfalls.**
- Attributing fever plus hypotension during a transfusion solely to sepsis or TRALI without checking for the hypertensive/volume-overload signs of TACO or the hypoxemia/infiltrates of TRALI can delay recognizing ABO-incompatible AHTR, which shares the febrile, hypotensive presentation but is confirmed by an ABO/clerical discrepancy plus a positive DAT.
- A negative pretransfusion antibody screen does not exclude prior alloimmunization: Kidd (Jk) antibodies are notorious for evanescing below detectable titers between exposures, then reappearing rapidly on re-exposure to cause DHTR — a newly positive screen or DAT after transfusion should always trigger a secondary-response workup.
- Autoadsorption to separate an autoantibody from a possible underlying alloantibody is invalid if the patient has been transfused within the last 3 months, since as little as 10% donor cells in circulation can adsorb out a clinically significant alloantibody and produce a false-negative screen; differential (allogeneic) adsorption must be used instead.
- A panreactive eluate (reactive with all panel cells, including the patient's own autologous cells) points toward a warm autoantibody rather than a discrete alloantibody; failing to distinguish panreactivity from cell-specific reactivity (e.g., Jk(a+)-restricted) risks misclassifying a DHTR as an autoimmune process, or vice versa.

## C-005 — Anemia in an AML Induction Patient: Transfusion Threshold, CMV-Safe Components, Irradiation, and the Storage Lesion  `C`
*1. Clinical Practice* · support: moderate

**Setup.** A 52-year-old woman with newly diagnosed acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is 5 days into cytarabine/anthracycline induction chemotherapy on the inpatient oncology service. She is CMV-seronegative and is a planned candidate for allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) once she achieves remission. Nursing reports new exertional dyspnea when she walks to the bathroom, though she remains hemodynamically stable at rest. Her most recent CBC shows a falling hemoglobin during her expected chemotherapy-induced pancytopenic nadir. The oncology team consults the transfusion medicine service for anemia and blood component support.

**Findings.**
- Hemoglobin, Day 5 of induction: 6.8 g/dL (abnormal)
- Heart rate: 88 bpm, regular (normal)
- Blood pressure: 118/72 mmHg (normal)
- Respiratory rate (at rest): 16/min (normal)
- Absolute neutrophil count: 310 cells/uL (critical)
- Platelet count: 42,000/uL (abnormal)
- Recipient CMV IgG serology: Negative (normal)
- Transplant plan: CMV-seronegative allogeneic HSCT candidate, pending remission
- Post-transfusion hemoglobin (after 2 units leukoreduced, irradiated RBCs): 8.5 g/dL (abnormal)
- 2,3-DPG level in stored donor RBC unit: Decreased (storage lesion) (abnormal)

**Decision points.**
1. Given her Hgb of 6.8 g/dL with new exertional dyspnea but hemodynamic stability at rest (no tachycardia, hypotension, or chest pain), what transfusion threshold and physiologic reasoning should guide the decision to transfuse RBCs now?
   - **Answer:** Apply the Hgb <7 g/dL threshold used in blood utilization review criteria for stable patients; transfuse now since she has crossed it and is symptomatic
   - Blood utilization audit/review criteria commonly use a threshold of Hgb <7 g/dL for stable patients to judge appropriateness of RBC transfusion. Compensatory mechanisms for anemia (increased cardiac output, increased RBC 2,3-DPG shifting the oxygen dissociation curve right to favor tissue O2 release, and redistribution of blood flow to vital organs) allow tolerance of anemia up to a point, but once the threshold is crossed and symptoms such as exertional dyspnea appear, transfusion is indicated even though the patient is stable at rest.
2. She is CMV-seronegative and being prepared for allogeneic HSCT. What RBC product characteristic should be specified to mitigate transfusion-transmitted CMV risk?
   - **Answer:** Prestorage-leukoreduced ('CMV-safe') RBC units are appropriate and have nearly replaced CMV-seronegative units in many institutions
   - CMV is a latent virus carried within monocytes and granulocytes, and mitigation is critical for at-risk populations including seronegative HSCT recipients. Prestorage leukocyte reduction (to <5x10^6 WBCs) effectively removes the CMV-carrying leukocytes; universal leukoreduction is considered 'CMV-safe' and has nearly replaced the use of seronegative units in many institutions, with true CMV-seronegative units reserved for highly specific protocols where 'safe' is not deemed sufficient.
3. Beyond CMV mitigation, does this patient's cellular blood component order need any additional processing given her clinical status, and why?
   - **Answer:** The units should be irradiated to inactivate donor T lymphocytes and prevent TA-GVHD
   - Transfusion-associated graft-versus-host disease (TA-GVHD) is prevented by gamma irradiation of cellular components, which inactivates donor T lymphocytes. A patient undergoing intensive AML induction chemotherapy and preparing for allogeneic HSCT is profoundly immunocompromised and cannot reject engrafting donor lymphocytes, making irradiated cellular components the appropriate order alongside leukoreduction.
4. After transfusion of 2 units of leukoreduced, irradiated RBCs her hemoglobin appropriately rises to 8.5 g/dL, but she still reports fatigue with minimal exertion in the first few hours afterward. What storage-related physiologic property of the transfused units best explains why immediate tissue oxygen delivery may lag behind the hemoglobin rise?
   - **Answer:** Stored RBC units have decreased 2,3-DPG, left-shifting the oxygen dissociation curve so hemoglobin holds oxygen more tightly and releases it less readily to tissue
   - A leftward shift of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve is caused by decreased 2,3-DPG (a recognized feature of the RBC storage lesion), along with decreased CO2, decreased temperature, or increased pH. A left-shifted curve means hemoglobin holds oxygen more tightly, potentially impairing tissue oxygen delivery immediately after transfusion even though the hemoglobin concentration itself has normalized.

**Key pathway.** Transfuse RBCs using the Hgb <7 g/dL restrictive threshold appropriate for a hemodynamically stable but now-symptomatic oncology patient; select prestorage-leukoreduced ("CMV-safe") RBC units given her CMV-seronegative, pre-allogeneic-HSCT status (rather than requiring true CMV-seronegative units); additionally irradiate all cellular components to inactivate donor T lymphocytes and prevent TA-GVHD given her profound treatment-related immunosuppression; and recognize that early post-transfusion fatigue can reflect the transient left-shifted oxyhemoglobin curve from storage-lesion 2,3-DPG depletion rather than an inadequate or failed transfusion.

**Teaching.** Anemia in oncology patients requires integrating red cell physiology with transfusion-safety decision-making rather than treating transfusion as a simple numbers exercise. Compensatory mechanisms -- increased cardiac output, increased 2,3-DPG, and redistribution of blood flow -- allow patients to tolerate progressive anemia, but restrictive institutional review criteria (Hgb <7 g/dL for stable patients) mark the point at which transfusion becomes the appropriate intervention rather than an optional one. Because many oncology patients are pre-HSCT or otherwise severely immunocompromised, component selection must also address CMV transmission risk (prestorage leukoreduction as the modern 'CMV-safe' standard, with true seronegative units reserved for narrower indications) and TA-GVHD risk (prevented by gamma irradiation, which inactivates donor T lymphocytes that could otherwise engraft in a host unable to mount a rejection response). Finally, the RBC storage lesion -- specifically depletion of 2,3-DPG -- left-shifts the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve in stored units, so a normalized hemoglobin value does not guarantee immediate normalization of tissue oxygen delivery.

**Pitfalls.**
- Treating the Hgb <7 g/dL figure as a rigid universal transfusion mandate rather than an audit/review criterion for stable patients -- symptoms and hemodynamic status still drive the actual clinical decision.
- Assuming true CMV-seronegative donor units are mandatory for every CMV-seronegative recipient -- prestorage leukoreduction is considered 'CMV-safe' and has nearly replaced seronegative units in many institutions except in highly specific protocols.
- Forgetting to order irradiated cellular components for severely immunocompromised oncology/HSCT patients, risking TA-GVHD from viable donor T lymphocytes that the host cannot reject.
- Expecting immediate, full restoration of tissue oxygen delivery the moment hemoglobin normalizes post-transfusion, without accounting for the left-shifted oxyhemoglobin curve caused by storage-lesion 2,3-DPG depletion.

## C-006 — Immune Platelet Transfusion Refractoriness: CCI, HLA Class I Alloantibodies, and Escalation to HPA Testing  `C`
*1. Clinical Practice* · support: high

**Setup.** A 34-year-old woman (G2P2, two prior term pregnancies) with newly diagnosed acute myeloid leukemia is undergoing induction chemotherapy and receiving prophylactic platelet support for severe thrombocytopenia. Despite receiving ABO-identical single-donor apheresis platelet units on consecutive days, she shows minimal rise in platelet count and develops scattered petechiae. She is intermittently febrile but hemodynamically stable, with no organomegaly on exam. The transfusion service is asked to determine whether she has developed platelet transfusion refractoriness and, if so, whether it is immune-mediated.

**Findings.**
- Platelet count, pre-transfusion (day 1): 8,000/µL (critical)
- Platelet count, 1 hour post-transfusion (unit 1; dose 3.0 ×10^11 platelets; BSA 1.8 m²): 13,000/µL (increment 5,000/µL) (abnormal)
- Calculated 1-hour CCI, transfusion 1: 3,000/µL (below the 5,000–7,500/µL threshold) (abnormal)
- Platelet count, pre-transfusion (day 2): 9,000/µL (critical)
- Platelet count, 1 hour post-transfusion (unit 2; same dose/BSA): 14,500/µL (increment 5,500/µL) (abnormal)
- Calculated 1-hour CCI, transfusion 2 (second consecutive low value): 3,300/µL (abnormal)
- ABO type: patient and platelet units: Group A patient; both apheresis units ABO-identical (Group A) (normal)
- Blood cultures ×2: No growth (normal)
- Coagulation panel (PT/PTT/fibrinogen/D-dimer): Within reference range, no DIC pattern (normal)
- Spleen exam/imaging: No splenomegaly (normal)
- Obstetric history: G2P2, two prior term pregnancies (HLA-sensitizing exposure)
- HLA antibody screen (solid-phase multiantigen bead/flow cytometric assay): Positive; broad reactivity against HLA-A and -B loci; elevated calculated PRA (cPRA) (abnormal)
- HLA Class II antibody testing: Negative (normal)
- 1-hour CCI after trial of HLA-matched (zero HLA-A/-B mismatch) apheresis unit: 3,500/µL — still below threshold (abnormal)
- Platelet-specific (HPA) antibody identification panel: Positive for anti-HPA-1a (critical)

**Decision points.**
1. Using the pre- and 1-hour post-transfusion counts, BSA (1.8 m²), and platelet dose (3.0 ×10^11) from transfusion 1, calculate the CCI and state what a single low value like this suggests about the etiology of her poor increment.
   - **Answer:** 1-hour CCI = (5,000/µL increment × 1.8 m²) / 3.0 (×10^11) = 3,000/µL. Because immune-mediated refractoriness is suggested by a 1-hour CCI <5,000–7,500/µL, this single low value raises concern for immune destruction, but the diagnostic criterion requires demonstrating this pattern on two consecutive transfusions before proceeding further.
   - SRC-026 defines the CCI formula (increment × BSA / platelets transfused in 10^11 units) and states that a 1-hour CCI <5,000–7,500/µL on two consecutive transfusions suggests immune refractoriness.
2. The following day's transfusion again yields a low 1-hour CCI (~3,300/µL), satisfying the two-consecutive-transfusion criterion. Blood cultures are negative, coagulation studies show no DIC pattern, there is no splenomegaly, and both units were ABO-identical. What has this evaluation accomplished before an HLA antibody workup is ordered?
   - **Answer:** It has excluded the major nonimmune causes of refractoriness (sepsis/DIC, splenomegaly, medication effect) that account for roughly 80% of cases, and it has ruled out ABO mismatch as a confounder of a poor increment — both necessary before attributing the poor CCI to alloimmunization.
   - SRC-026 notes that approximately 80% of refractoriness is nonimmune (sepsis, DIC, splenomegaly, medications), and that ABO-mismatched platelets (e.g., Group A platelets to a Group O recipient) can also blunt increments and should be checked before concluding immune refractoriness.
3. With immune refractoriness now supported, what test should be ordered, what antigens does it target, and how should a positive HLA Class II result on the same panel be interpreted?
   - **Answer:** Order HLA antibody screening by a solid-phase (multiantigen bead/flow cytometric) assay to determine the patient's panel-reactive antibody (PRA/cPRA). The primary target in immune platelet refractoriness is HLA Class I (HLA-A and -B), the antigens expressed on the platelet surface. Platelets do not express HLA Class II, so a positive Class II result reflects sensitization to contaminating leukocytes in prior products rather than antibody directed at the platelets themselves. Her obstetric history (G2P2) is clinically relevant, as pregnancy is the most significant risk factor for primary HLA sensitization.
   - SRC-026 describes HLA screening via multiantigen-coated bead flow cytometry to determine PRA/cPRA, identifies HLA Class I (A, B) as the primary immune target, flags Class II positivity as a leukocyte-sensitization distractor, and names pregnancy as the leading HLA-sensitizing risk factor. SRC-028 and SRC-010 corroborate that platelet/nucleated-cell expression is restricted to Class I (A, B, C) while Class II is restricted to antigen-presenting cells.
4. The HLA screen shows broad HLA-A/-B reactivity with elevated cPRA. What platelet-selection strategy should be used, what safety step is mandatory for the selected product, and what should be done because she remains refractory despite a trial of HLA-matched platelets?
   - **Answer:** Select platelets via HLA matching (identical or zero-mismatch HLA-A/-B donors) or, as an equally effective and often faster alternative, HLA-antibody avoidance using a virtual crossmatch (ASP method — selecting donors lacking antigens corresponding to her antibody specificities) or physical platelet crossmatching (solid-phase red cell adherence assay). Any HLA-selected or crossmatched unit must be irradiated, because the high degree of donor-recipient matching increases the risk of donor T-cell engraftment and TA-GVHD. Since her CCI remains low despite an HLA-matched unit, proceed to platelet-specific (HPA) antibody testing; anti-HPA-1a is identified, so she now requires HPA antigen-negative platelets.
   - SRC-026 lists HLA-matched platelets, HLA-antibody avoidance via virtual crossmatch (ASP, 'as effective as matching'), and SPRCA platelet crossmatching as management options; mandates irradiation of HLA-selected/crossmatched platelets to prevent TA-GVHD; and states that antigen-negative (HPA) selection is required if refractoriness persists with HLA-compatible units and a platelet-specific antibody such as anti-HPA-1a is identified. SRC-006 adds that platelets carry ABO, HLA Class I, and HPA antigen systems, and that HPA antibodies are also relevant to FNAIT and post-transfusion purpura.

**Key pathway.** Two consecutive 1-hour CCIs <5,000–7,500/µL, obtained only after excluding the ~80% nonimmune causes of refractoriness (sepsis/DIC, splenomegaly, medication effect) and confirming ABO-identical product, establish immune platelet refractoriness. Solid-phase HLA antibody screening demonstrates HLA Class I (A/B) reactivity with an elevated cPRA — consistent with prior pregnancy as her primary HLA-sensitizing exposure — while a negative Class II result confirms the antibodies are platelet-directed rather than reflecting leukocyte sensitization. Management proceeds to HLA-matched platelets or, as an equally effective (and often faster) alternative, antibody-specificity-based avoidance via virtual crossmatch (ASP method) or physical platelet crossmatching (SPRCA); any HLA-selected/crossmatched product must be irradiated to prevent TA-GVHD. Because refractoriness persists despite HLA-compatible platelets, the workup escalates to platelet-specific (HPA) antibody testing, anti-HPA-1a is identified, and HPA antigen-negative platelets are required.

**Teaching.** Immune-mediated platelet destruction spans a spectrum from autoimmune ITP to alloimmune transfusion refractoriness, and the source curriculum groups HLA/HPA antibody assays under this Immune Thrombocytopenia node because they are the workhorse tests for investigating platelet destruction in multiply transfused patients. The diagnostic logic is kinetic before it is serologic: the 1-hour CCI (increment × BSA / platelets transfused in 10^11) separates immune destruction (poor 1-hour recovery) from nonimmune consumption (acceptable 1-hour recovery with decay by 18–24 hours), and a threshold of <5,000–7,500/µL on two consecutive transfusions is the trigger for an immune workup — but only after the ~80% of nonimmune causes and ABO mismatch are excluded. Serologic confirmation targets HLA Class I (A, B), the only HLA class platelets express; a positive Class II result is a leukocyte-sensitization distractor, not evidence of platelet-directed disease. Management is stepwise: HLA-matched or antibody-avoidance (virtual crossmatch/ASP) or crossmatched platelets first, always irradiated because close HLA matching raises TA-GVHD risk, and HPA antigen-negative platelets reserved for refractoriness that persists despite HLA-compatible product and a demonstrated platelet-specific antibody (e.g., anti-HPA-1a).

**Pitfalls.**
- Ordering an HLA antibody workup before excluding the ~80% of refractoriness cases that are nonimmune (sepsis/DIC, splenomegaly, medications) — the CCI pattern alone does not replace this clinical exclusion.
- Misreading a positive HLA Class II antibody as evidence of platelet-directed alloimmunization — platelets do not express Class II, so a positive result reflects sensitization to contaminating leukocytes, a classic board distractor.
- Forgetting that HLA-matched, ASP-selected, or crossmatched platelet units still require irradiation, since the closer donor-recipient HLA match paradoxically raises the risk of donor T-cell engraftment and TA-GVHD.
- Confusing the timing pattern (a good 1-hour count with a poor 24-hour count indicates nonimmune consumption, not HLA-antibody-mediated destruction) and failing to confirm ABO-identical/compatible product before attributing a poor increment to alloimmunization.

## C-007 — New Thrombocytopenia on Heparin: Immune Thrombocytopenia (HIT/ITP) vs. TTP and Why TPE Is Not Reflexive  `C`
*1. Clinical Practice* · support: low

**Setup.** A 64-year-old man is postoperative day 8 after major surgery and has been maintained on a continuous unfractionated heparin infusion for venous thromboembolism prophylaxis. His platelet count, previously stable, has fallen, prompting an urgent hematology/transfusion medicine consult. There is no bleeding. The team must reason through whether this represents an antibody-mediated (immune) thrombocytopenic process — with heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) in the differential — versus thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP), and whether therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) should be started empirically while confirmatory testing is pending.

**Findings.**
- Platelet count trend: 142,000/µL on admission → 58,000/µL by hospital day 8, on ongoing unfractionated heparin infusion (abnormal)
- Fever: Absent; patient afebrile (normal)
- Neurologic examination: Nonfocal; no altered mental status (normal)
- Renal function (creatinine): Within normal limits, unchanged from baseline (normal)
- Hemolysis markers / peripheral smear: No schistocytosis; LDH and haptoglobin unremarkable (normal)
- Bleeding: No mucocutaneous or overt bleeding (normal)
- TTP pentad criteria met: 1 of 5 (thrombocytopenia only); microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, neurologic signs, fever, and renal dysfunction all absent (normal)
- ADAMTS13 activity: Sent, result pending

**Decision points.**
1. A hospitalized patient on heparin develops new, unexplained thrombocytopenia. What is the correct first step in clinical reasoning?
   - **Answer:** Characterize the thrombocytopenia by underlying mechanism (immune vs. nonimmune) before committing to a specific treatment pathway
   - The approved curriculum organizes hematologic/transfusion-related processes by underlying pathophysiologic mechanism: SRC-045 frames transfusion reaction classification explicitly around Immune vs. Nonimmune mechanism, and SRC-010 places Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT) within the 'Immune Thrombocytopenia' clinical-practice subdomain, paired with ITP. Before a specific management pathway (e.g., TTP-directed ADAMTS13/TPE workup, or an immune-thrombocytopenia antibody-assay workup) can be chosen, the mechanism must be established rather than assumed.
2. The patient is afebrile, has a nonfocal neurologic exam, normal renal function, and no schistocytosis or hemolysis markers -- only isolated thrombocytopenia. How should this shape the differential relative to a TTP-suspected patient?
   - **Answer:** Because the patient does not exhibit the TTP pentad (MAHA, thrombocytopenia, neurologic signs, fever, renal dysfunction), the TTP-specific pathway (PLASMIC score / ADAMTS13-directed management) is lower yield; proceed down the immune thrombocytopenia (ITP/HIT) antibody-assay pathway instead
   - SRC-023 defines the TTP pentad as microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA), thrombocytopenia, neurologic signs, fever, and renal dysfunction, and describes the PLASMIC score as a tool used to discriminate TTP from other microangiopathies specifically while awaiting ADAMTS13 results -- i.e., interim reasoning before ADAMTS13 returns is exactly the intended use case. A patient with isolated thrombocytopenia and none of the other pentad features is a poor fit for the TTP pathway, supporting evaluation within the curriculum's immune thrombocytopenia (ITP/HIT) category instead (SRC-010).
3. Should therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) be started empirically for this heparin-associated thrombocytopenia while confirmatory antibody testing is pending?
   - **Answer:** No -- the approved apheresis source lists ASFA Category I (accepted, first-line) indications as conditions such as TTP and Guillain-Barré syndrome; it does not establish immune thrombocytopenia (ITP/HIT) as a TPE indication, and TPE's proven TTP mechanism (removing the ADAMTS13 inhibitor/ultra-large vWF multimers while replacing ADAMTS13) does not apply here
   - SRC-023 states ASFA Category I disorders are those for which apheresis is accepted as first-line therapy, giving TTP and Guillain-Barré syndrome as examples; it does not list HIT or ITP among these. The same source explains TPE is first-line for TTP specifically because it removes the ADAMTS13 inhibitor and ultra-large vWF multimers while replacing the deficient protease -- a mechanism tied to TTP pathophysiology, not to an antibody-mediated platelet disorder in general. The fact that a 1.0-PV exchange removes ~63-70% of an intravascular substance (SRC-023) describes removal kinetics, not clinical indication, and is a common board distractor.
4. What laboratory approach does the approved curriculum indicate for confirming an immune thrombocytopenia diagnosis such as HIT, and what is a key limitation of these sources on this specific point?
   - **Answer:** The curriculum states antibody-based assays (explicitly, 'HLA and HPA antibody assays' for ITP investigation) are the relevant category of laboratory testing for the paired immune thrombocytopenia entities (ITP/HIT); however, the approved sources do not name the specific confirmatory assay(s) used for HIT itself
   - SRC-010 states that 'ITP involves platelet destruction where HLA and HPA antibody assays are critical for investigation' and separately groups 'classification and pathogenesis of Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP) and Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT)' together as a Domain I high-yield concept. This establishes that the diagnostic paradigm for this category is antibody-based laboratory testing rather than the pentad/PLASMIC/ADAMTS13/TPE pathway used for TTP -- but the approved source set does not go on to name the specific confirmatory assay(s) used for HIT, which is a genuine gap in this particular source set that the learner should recognize rather than fill in with unverified outside content. The BNP/NT-proBNP ≥1.5x and PaO2/FiO2 ≤300 criteria (SRC-050) are specific to TACO/TRALI diagnosis, not to thrombocytopenia workups, and are a distractor here.

**Key pathway.** New thrombocytopenia in a hospitalized patient on heparin must first be classified by mechanism (immune vs. nonimmune) rather than assigned a treatment reflexively. Because this patient lacks the TTP pentad (MAHA, neurologic signs, fever, renal dysfunction) and the clinical picture does not fit the ADAMTS13/PLASMIC-driven TTP pathway, the case is worked up as an immune-mediated thrombocytopenia in the curriculum's paired ITP/HIT category, using antibody-based laboratory assays — the investigative modality the approved curriculum specifies for that category — rather than starting therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE). TPE is not initiated empirically: the approved apheresis source restricts ASFA Category I (accepted, first-line) apheresis indications to conditions such as TTP and Guillain-Barré syndrome and does not establish immune thrombocytopenia (ITP/HIT) as a TPE indication, and TPE's proven TTP-specific mechanism (removing the ADAMTS13 inhibitor and ultra-large vWF multimers while replacing ADAMTS13) does not carry over to an antibody-mediated platelet disorder outside that mechanism.

**Teaching.** The approved board curriculum (SRC-010) groups Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT) with Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP) under the Clinical Practice domain, framing both as immune/antibody-mediated platelet destruction whose investigation centers on antibody-based assays (stated explicitly for ITP). This case teaches the surrounding clinical reasoning that the approved sources do support well: (1) hematologic and transfusion-related processes should be classified by mechanism -- immune vs. nonimmune -- before a management pathway is chosen (SRC-045); (2) thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) is a distinct, richly source-documented entity with its own pentad (MAHA, thrombocytopenia, neurologic signs, fever, renal dysfunction), the PLASMIC score, ADAMTS13-directed pathophysiology, and first-line therapeutic plasma exchange (SRC-023); and (3) apheresis is not a generic antibody-removal tool to be reached for whenever an antibody-mediated process is suspected -- ASFA Category I (first-line, accepted) indications are specific (e.g., TTP, Guillain-Barré syndrome), and the source materials explicitly warn about over-extending apheresis to conditions where it is ineffective or unproven (SRC-023's Category IV distractor teaching pearl). Honesty about source limits is itself part of the lesson: these four approved sources establish that HIT exists as a named, antibody-mediated immune thrombocytopenia paired with ITP, but they do not provide the specific confirmatory laboratory assay(s) for HIT (e.g., antigen- or function-based platelet-antibody testing). A learner using only this source set should be able to correctly triage the case away from TTP/apheresis and toward an antibody-assay-based ITP/HIT pathway, while recognizing that assay-level HIT detail would require material beyond these four documents.

**Pitfalls.**
- Assuming any antibody-mediated cytopenia automatically qualifies for therapeutic plasma exchange -- the ASFA Category I list is specific (e.g., TTP, Guillain-Barré syndrome), not an open-ended category for 'antibodies are pathogenic, so remove them.'
- Misapplying pulmonary-reaction-specific criteria (BNP/NT-proBNP ≥1.5x for TACO, PaO2/FiO2 ≤300 for TRALI/TACO hypoxemia) to an unrelated thrombocytopenia workup -- these thresholds are defined in the sources strictly for pulmonary transfusion reactions.
- Treating the fact that a 1.0-plasma-volume exchange removes ~63-70% of an intravascular substance as proof of clinical efficacy or indication, rather than recognizing it as a description of extracorporeal removal kinetics that applies regardless of whether treatment is actually indicated.
- Assuming the approved source set provides a full HIT confirmatory-assay algorithm -- it only establishes HIT's classification alongside ITP as an immune thrombocytopenia and the general antibody-assay paradigm (stated for ITP); board-level HIT assay specifics are not present in these four sources and should not be presented as if they were.

**Sources:** `SRC-010`, `SRC-023`, `SRC-045`, `SRC-050`

## C-008 — Anemia of Prematurity: The Blunted Erythropoietin Response in a Very Preterm Neonate  `C`
*3. RBCs and RBC Components* · support: high

**Setup.** A former 28-week-gestation infant (birth weight 1100 g), now day of life 21 in the NICU (postmenstrual age ~31 weeks), develops new tachycardia, tachypnea, and poor weight gain over several days, with an increasing supplemental oxygen requirement. The infant has undergone numerous heel-stick and venous draws for routine NICU monitoring but has no history of overt bleeding. The prenatal maternal antibody screen and the infant's initial newborn antibody screen were both negative.

**Findings.**
- Gestational age at birth: 28 weeks
- Birth weight: 1100 g (<1200 g) (abnormal)
- Postnatal age: Day of life 21 (postmenstrual age ~31 weeks)
- Heart rate: 168 bpm (abnormal)
- Respiratory rate: 68/min (abnormal)
- Hemoglobin: 7.9 g/dL (abnormal)
- Absolute reticulocyte count: Low, inappropriately reduced for the degree of anemia (abnormal)
- Serum erythropoietin (if measured): Inappropriately low/blunted relative to the degree of anemia (abnormal)
- Direct antiglobulin test (DAT): Negative (normal)
- Total/unconjugated bilirubin: Not significantly elevated, age-appropriate (normal)
- Peripheral smear: No significant spherocytosis or erythroblastosis (normal)
- Maternal antibody screen (prenatal): Negative (normal)
- Initial newborn antibody screen: Negative (normal)
- History of overt bleeding/hemorrhage: None reported (normal)

**Decision points.**
1. The infant is anemic and symptomatic (tachycardia, tachypnea, poor weight gain, rising oxygen requirement), yet the reticulocyte count remains inappropriately low rather than showing a robust regenerative response. What is the underlying physiologic explanation for this hyporegenerative pattern in a neonate this age?
   - **Answer:** Blunted erythropoietin (EPO) response to anemia, a physiologic feature of neonates less than 4 months of age
   - Red cell production is regulated by erythropoietin (SRC-024). Neonates under 4 months have immature organ systems, small blood volumes (~85-100 mL/kg), and a blunted EPO response to anemia (SRC-017, SRC-025), so the marrow signal that would normally drive reticulocytosis is attenuated, producing a hyporegenerative anemia despite clinical symptoms of decompensation.
2. Before attributing this anemia to prematurity-related hyporegeneration, the team wants to exclude an immune hemolytic process such as HDFN. Which combination of findings supports a non-hemolytic, hyporegenerative etiology rather than HDFN?
   - **Answer:** Negative DAT, non-elevated bilirubin, no significant erythroblastosis or spherocytosis, and negative maternal/newborn antibody screens
   - Newborn HDFN workup classically shows a positive DAT (anti-IgG), elevated unconjugated bilirubin, and characteristic morphology - spherocytes predominate in ABO HDFN while erythroblastosis predominates in Rh HDFN (SRC-017, SRC-025). The absence of all of these, combined with negative maternal and newborn antibody screens, argues against an immune hemolytic process and is consistent with a hyporegenerative, EPO-driven anemia of prematurity instead.
3. The infant (birth weight 1100 g) remains symptomatic and the team decides to transfuse. What unit specifications, volume strategy, and pretransfusion testing approach are appropriate?
   - **Answer:** Transfuse an irradiated, leukoreduced (CMV-safe-equivalent), small-volume aliquot (10-15 mL/kg) from a dedicated donor unit; because the initial antibody screen was negative and only group O compatible cells are used, repeat ABO/Rh typing and antibody screens are not required for the rest of the admission
   - Irradiation is mandatory for premature neonates <1200 g to prevent TA-GVHD (SRC-017, SRC-025). Universal leukoreduction is considered equivalent to CMV-seronegative status for preventing CMV transmission to neonates (SRC-025). Simple neonatal transfusions use small-volume aliquots (10-15 mL/kg) from dedicated units to minimize donor exposure (SRC-017). Because the neonate's initial antibody screen was negative and only group O compatible cells are being given, repeat ABO/Rh typing and antibody screens may be omitted for the entire admission through 4 months of age (SRC-017, SRC-025).

**Key pathway.** Recognize that the anemic, symptomatic, hyporegenerative (low-reticulocyte) picture in this very preterm neonate reflects the physiologic blunted erythropoietin (EPO) response to anemia seen in neonates under 4 months of age - i.e., anemia of prematurity - rather than an ongoing hemolytic process. Exclude HDFN using a negative DAT, non-elevated bilirubin, unremarkable smear (no spherocytosis/erythroblastosis), and negative maternal/newborn antibody screens. Manage with an irradiated (mandatory because birth weight <1200 g, to prevent TA-GVHD), leukoreduced (CMV-safe-equivalent), small-volume aliquot (10-15 mL/kg) from a dedicated donor unit to limit donor exposure; because the initial antibody screen was negative and only group O compatible cells are used, ABO/Rh typing and antibody screens do not need to be repeated for the remainder of the admission (through 4 months of age).

**Teaching.** Erythropoietin is the principal regulator of red cell production, driving marrow reticulocytosis in response to tissue hypoxia (SRC-024; EPO regulation is listed as a Major Concept under the RBCs and RBC Components domain in SRC-010). In neonates under 4 months of age, this feedback loop is physiologically blunted - the EPO response to anemia is attenuated compared to older infants and adults - so a preterm neonate can become significantly anemic and symptomatic (tachycardia with pulse >100, tachypnea with respiration >30, poor weight gain, increased oxygen requirement) without mounting an adequate reticulocyte response (SRC-017, SRC-025). This hyporegenerative pattern, termed anemia of prematurity, must be distinguished from an actively hemolytic process such as HDFN, which instead produces a positive DAT, elevated bilirubin, and characteristic morphology (spherocytes in ABO HDFN, erythroblastosis in Rh HDFN). Once a hemolytic etiology is excluded, management of symptomatic anemia of prematurity draws on neonate-specific transfusion practice rather than adult transfusion rules: irradiation is mandatory below 1200 g birth weight to prevent TA-GVHD, leukoreduction substitutes for CMV-seronegative status, small-volume aliquots from a dedicated unit limit donor exposure given the neonate's small blood volume, and - if the initial screen was negative and only group O compatible cells are used - repeat compatibility testing can be safely omitted for the remainder of the admission.

**Pitfalls.**
- Misreading a hyporegenerative (low) reticulocyte response in a preterm neonate as bone marrow failure or occult hemolysis instead of recognizing the physiologic blunted EPO response characteristic of neonates under 4 months (SRC-017, SRC-025).
- Extrapolating adult restrictive-transfusion trigger evidence (TRICC, FOCUS; ~7-8 g/dL for stable patients, SRC-024) directly onto neonatal decision-making without accounting for neonates' distinct EPO physiology, small blood volume, and symptom burden (SRC-017, SRC-025).
- Forgetting mandatory irradiation for an infant under 1200 g at birth, risking TA-GVHD from a non-irradiated cellular component (SRC-017, SRC-025).
- Reflexively repeating ABO/Rh typing and antibody screens on every neonatal transfusion encounter, when source-grounded practice allows omission through 4 months of age if the initial screen was negative and only group O compatible cells are used (SRC-017, SRC-025).

**Setup.** A 54-year-old man with hereditary hemochromatosis presents to the transfusion medicine/apheresis unit for the fourth session of a planned therapeutic phlebotomy series. 2. The apheresis medical director is asked whether this therapeutic phlebotomy order needs a formal ASFA category (I-IV) assignment before it can be scheduled, the way a TTP plasma exchange or a sickle cell red cell exchange would. How should this be answered?
   - **Answer:** No - apheresis is defined as blood passed through a medical device to separate and return components, and therapeutic phlebotomy has no separation device and no component return, so it sits outside the ASFA category framework
   - The approved source defines apheresis as a procedure in which blood is passed through a medical device to separate one or more components and return the remainder to the patient, and frames ASFA Category I-IV rankings around exactly that kind of device-based indication (e.g., TPE for TTP as Category I, red cell exchange in sickle cell disease). **Teaching.** Board questions on therapeutic phlebotomy frequently test whether the candidate can separate it, both conceptually and administratively, from device-based therapeutic apheresis. Apheresis is explicitly defined as passing blood through a device that separates and returns components, and the ASFA category system (I-IV) is built around exactly that kind of procedure; a manual whole-blood collection - even when performed for a therapeutic indication such as iron overload - does not fit that framework and needs no ASFA justification. **Pitfalls.**
- Conflating "apheresis" with "phlebotomy" and inappropriately trying to assign an ASFA category (I-IV) to a manual therapeutic phlebotomy order, when ASFA logic governs device-based separation-and-return procedures.
- Attributing a vasovagal reaction's symptoms (or any perioral tingling) to citrate toxicity during therapeutic phlebotomy - citrate toxicity requires an anticoagulated, extracorporeal component-return circuit that simple whole-blood phlebotomy does not have.
- Proceeding with a scheduled phlebotomy session purely "by protocol" without reassessing hemoglobin/hematocrit and clinical symptoms, risking iatrogenic anemia from cumulative blood removal.
- Over-grading a rapidly self-limited vasovagal episode (resolves in seconds with repositioning, no lasting sequelae) as a severe (Grade 3 or higher) event; standardized criteria reserve higher grades for events requiring hospitalization, prolonged disability (>6 months), or life-threatening intervention.

## C-012 — Bringing a New Apheresis Cell Separator Online: Validation, QC, and Cold-Chain Technology for HPC-A Collection  `AR`
*5. Apheresis* · support: high

**Setup.** A 58-year-old man with multiple myeloma in first remission is scheduled for autologous peripheral blood stem cell (HPC-A) collection by apheresis in preparation for an autologous transplant. The cellular therapy laboratory has just installed a new apheresis cell separator to perform the collection. Across the collection, processing, and preservation steps, the quality manager and technologists must make a series of device- and technology-governance decisions to keep the product safe and usable for eventual infusion.

**Findings.**
- New apheresis cell separator validation status: Installation Qualification (IQ) complete per manufacturer specification; Operational Qualification (OQ) and Performance Qualification (PQ) not yet performed; no Medical Director sign-off on the validation summary (abnormal)
- HPC-A collection outcome: Product collected via the new apheresis instrument; volume and gross appearance acceptable (normal)
- Post-collection release QC (TNC count, CD34+ enumeration, viability assay, microbial sterility): Not yet performed; technologist proposes proceeding directly to cryopreservation (abnormal)
- Proposed processing environment for volume reduction / CD34+ selection: Open bench, outside any biological safety cabinet (abnormal)
- Cryopreservation method: DMSO-based cryoprotectant with controlled-rate cooling (normal)

**Decision points.**
1. The new apheresis cell separator has been installed and calibrated by the vendor per manufacturer specification. Is it ready to collect this patient's clinical HPC-A product?
   - **Answer:** No - Operational Qualification (OQ) and Performance Qualification (PQ) must also be completed, with final Medical Director approval of the validation summary, before clinical use
   - Process validation for new equipment/technology requires three phases: IQ (installed per specifications in an appropriate environment), OQ (process operates as intended, including worst-case challenges), and PQ (consistently performs under normal working conditions). The Medical Director must give final approval of the validation summary before the process or equipment is implemented for clinical use.
2. The HPC-A product has been successfully collected. What must be completed before the product can be released for cryopreservation?
   - **Answer:** Total nucleated cell (TNC) count, CD34+ enumeration, viability assay, and microbial sterility testing
   - Quality control on HPC-A products mandates assessment of total nucleated cell (TNC) counts, CD34+ enumeration, viability assays, and microbial sterility before the product is considered releasable.
3. The technologist plans to perform volume reduction and CD34+ selection on the open collection bag on an open bench to save time. Is this acceptable?
   - **Answer:** Not acceptable - a Class II Biological Safety Cabinet is required, because it protects personnel, environment, AND the product
   - Class II BSCs protect personnel, environment, and product and are the standard for blood bank open-processing and cellular therapy; this aligns with BSL-2 containment requirements that govern most blood bank/cellular therapy laboratory activities.
4. The product must stay viably preserved for about 3 weeks until the patient completes conditioning chemotherapy. The technologist cryopreserves it with a DMSO-based cryoprotectant using controlled-rate cooling, then proposes storing/shipping it on dry ice (-78.5°C) for the entire 3-week interval for convenience. Is this appropriate?
   - **Answer:** No - long-term frozen storage should default to liquid nitrogen vapor phase (-196°C); dry ice is appropriate only if specifically validated for that shorter duration
   - Cryopreservation uses cryoprotectants (e.g., DMSO) and controlled-rate cooling to prevent cellular damage. Maintenance of the cold chain is critical: products must be transported in liquid nitrogen vapor phase (-196°C) for frozen storage, or via dry ice (-78.5°C) only if specifically validated for shorter durations, followed by bedside thawing and immediate infusion with rigorous monitoring for adverse reactions.

**Key pathway.** Correct pathway: (1) A newly installed apheresis cell separator cannot be used clinically on installation/calibration alone — full three-phase validation (IQ → OQ → PQ) must be completed, with final Medical Director approval of the validation summary, before it collects a clinical HPC-A product. (2) After collection, the product cannot go straight to cryopreservation — mandatory release QC (TNC count, CD34+ enumeration, viability assay, microbial sterility) must be performed first. (3) Any open manipulation of the product (volume reduction, CD34+ selection) must occur inside a Class II Biological Safety Cabinet, which protects personnel, environment, AND product (not a Class I, which protects only personnel/environment) — consistent with BSL-2 standards for blood bank/cellular therapy processing. (4) Cryopreservation uses a DMSO-based cryoprotectant with controlled-rate cooling; for a multi-week hold, the product must be maintained in liquid nitrogen vapor phase (-196°C) for reliable long-term frozen storage — dry ice (-78.5°C) is acceptable only if specifically validated for that shorter duration — followed by bedside thaw and immediate infusion with rigorous monitoring for adverse reactions.

**Teaching.** In modern cellular therapy/apheresis programs, "current devices and technology" is inseparable from the quality management system that governs them. Bringing a new apheresis cell separator online is not complete at installation: the facility must walk through the full three-phase validation pathway (IQ confirms correct installation and environment; OQ demonstrates the process works as intended including worst-case challenges; PQ confirms consistent performance under real clinical conditions), and the Medical Director must sign off before the instrument touches a clinical collection. Once a product is collected, release is gated on defined QC (TNC, CD34+, viability, sterility) — QC is not optional or deferrable to "whenever convenient." Any open manipulation of the product (volume reduction, CD34+ selection, T-cell depletion) must happen inside a Class II BSC, the blood bank/cellular-therapy standard, because it uniquely protects the product itself in addition to personnel and environment (unlike Class I). Finally, the preservation technology chain — DMSO cryoprotectant plus controlled-rate cooling, then a validated cold chain (LN2 vapor phase at -196°C for standard frozen storage, dry ice at -78.5°C only when validated for a shorter interval) — determines whether the product remains viable through bedside thaw and infusion. Ongoing equipment QC (e.g., centrifuge speed/timer checked quarterly, centrifuge function checked yearly) keeps an apheresis instrument in a state of control after initial validation. Related but distinct "current technology," pathogen inactivation systems (amotosalen/UVA, riboflavin/UV, UVC) apply this same technology-governance mindset to apheresis-collected platelet and plasma components rather than to cellular therapy products.

**Pitfalls.**
- Assuming installation and vendor calibration (IQ) alone qualify a new apheresis device for clinical use; OQ, PQ, and Medical Director sign-off on the validation summary are also required.
- Confusing BSC classes: selecting a Class I cabinet (protects personnel/environment only) when Class II is required because it also protects the product, which is essential for open HPC-A processing.
- Treating dry ice (-78.5°C) as an unrestricted substitute for liquid nitrogen vapor phase (-196°C) storage; dry ice is only appropriate for durations specifically validated for it.
- Proceeding to cryopreservation or infusion before mandatory release QC (TNC, CD34+ enumeration, viability, sterility) is completed and documented.

## C-013 — Early Neonatal Jaundice in a First Pregnancy: Recognizing ABO HDFN  `C`
*15. Obstetric and Pediatric Patients* · support: high

**Setup.** A 20-hour-old term male infant develops visible jaundice on the newborn nursery. He was delivered vaginally without complication to a 28-year-old G1P1 mother (this is her first pregnancy) whose prenatal blood type is O, Rh-positive, with a documented negative antibody screen at her first prenatal visit and again at admission for delivery. The infant is well-appearing but visibly icteric on exam, without hepatosplenomegaly, and there were no signs of hydrops fetalis on prior prenatal ultrasounds. The infant's blood type returns as A, Rh-positive.

**Findings.**
- Maternal ABO/Rh type: Group O, Rh-positive
- Maternal antibody screen (prenatal and at delivery): Negative (normal)
- Obstetric history: G1P1, first pregnancy, uncomplicated
- Infant ABO/Rh type: Group A, Rh-positive
- Onset of jaundice: ~20 hours of life (abnormal)
- Newborn direct antiglobulin test (DAT): Weakly positive, anti-IgG (abnormal)
- Newborn hemoglobin/hematocrit at birth: Within normal limits for gestational age (normal)
- Total/unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin: Elevated, unconjugated fraction predominant (abnormal)
- Peripheral blood smear: Spherocytes present; no significant erythroblastosis (nucleated RBCs not prominent) (abnormal)
- Prenatal ultrasound findings: No hydrops fetalis, no ascites/edema (normal)

**Decision points.**
1. Given the mother is O-positive with a negative antibody screen and this is her first pregnancy, what is the most likely cause of this newborn's early jaundice, and how can it occur in a first pregnancy at all?
   - **Answer:** ABO HDFN due to naturally occurring maternal anti-A
   - Anti-A and anti-B are naturally occurring antibodies, not induced by prior antigen exposure, so ABO HDFN — unlike Rh HDFN, which requires prior maternal sensitization — can occur in a first pregnancy (SRC-017: 'ABO HDFN Timing: Unlike Rh HDFN, ABO HDFN can occur in the first pregnancy because anti-A and anti-B are naturally occurring'). The mother's negative antibody screen does not exclude this: prenatal antibody screens are performed against reagent O-cell panels (SRC-024: antibody screening uses 'reagent O-cell sets'), which lack A and B antigens and therefore cannot detect anti-A/anti-B.
2. The newborn's labs show a weakly positive DAT, normal Hgb/Hct at birth, elevated unconjugated bilirubin, and spherocytes on smear without significant erythroblastosis. How does this pattern support ABO HDFN over Rh HDFN, and what does it imply about severity?
   - **Answer:** This pattern is classic for ABO HDFN and supports a mild clinical course
   - Per SRC-017's comparison point, 'Rh is typically severe (anemia at birth); ABO is typically mild (normal hemoglobin at birth, delayed jaundice)' — this infant's normal birth Hgb fits ABO HDFN, not Rh HDFN. Morphologically, 'spherocytes are common in ABO HDFN but rare in Rh HDFN (where erythroblasts predominate)' (SRC-017), matching this smear. The only weakly positive DAT is also consistent with the milder ABO picture, paralleling the recognized phenomenon that ABO antigen expression/reactivity is comparatively weak in newborns (SRC-024: 'Missing/Weak antigens: Seen in newborns').
3. With no anemia at birth, no hydrops, and isolated elevated unconjugated bilirubin, what is the appropriate first-line management, and what is the rationale for treating at all?
   - **Answer:** Phototherapy (460–490 nm) as first-line, with IVIG as an adjunct if bilirubin continues to rise
   - SRC-017 lists neonatal HDFN therapy as 'Phototherapy (460–490 nm) to conjugate bilirubin; IVIG to block Fc receptors; or double-volume exchange transfusion to remove bilirubin and antibodies' — phototherapy is the appropriate first step for a mild presentation, with IVIG reserved for bilirubin trending toward exchange-level concern. Treatment is warranted because unconjugated bilirubin, if allowed to rise unchecked, risks kernicterus, defined in SRC-017 as 'permanent brain damage caused by toxic levels of unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin in the neonatal brain.'
4. Suppose bilirubin continued to rise despite intensive phototherapy and IVIG, requiring double-volume exchange transfusion. What special requirements apply to the red cell component, and what is a common pitfall when interpreting this newborn's initial ABO/Rh typing?
   - **Answer:** Components must be irradiated and CMV-reduced-risk; a weak/missing antigen reaction on newborn typing should not automatically be read as a true discrepancy or as 'blocked' antigen sites
   - SRC-017 states irradiation is 'Mandatory for IUT, exchange transfusion, and premature neonates (<1200g) to prevent TA-GVHD,' and SRC-010 notes neonates (<4 months) 'require specific blood banking practices, such as the use of dedicated units or CMV-reduced components.' Separately, SRC-024 identifies 'Missing/Weak antigens' as an expected ABO discrepancy pattern 'seen in newborns' due to immature antigen expression — this is a distinct, benign phenomenon from the 'blocked Rh' false-negative typing caused by heavy maternal anti-D coating D-antigen sites, which SRC-017 notes is 'resolved by performing an eluate.' Conflating ordinary newborn antigen weakness with true serologic discrepancy or with blocked-antigen phenomena is a recognized board trap.

**Key pathway.** ABO HDFN in an O-mother/A(or B)-infant pair presenting as mild, early (12–48h) jaundice in a FIRST pregnancy — possible because anti-A/anti-B are naturally occurring IgG1/IgG3 antibodies that do not require prior sensitization (unlike Rh HDFN). The lab picture (weakly positive DAT, normal-to-mildly-low Hgb at birth, spherocytes rather than erythroblasts on smear) confirms a mild extravascular hemolytic process typical of ABO HDFN rather than the more severe, anemia-at-birth, erythroblast-predominant picture of Rh HDFN. First-line management is phototherapy (460–490 nm) ± IVIG to block Fc receptors; double-volume exchange transfusion is reserved for bilirubin refractory to these measures, and any cellular component given to the neonate (<4 months) must be irradiated and CMV-reduced-risk.

**Teaching.** HDFN results from maternal IgG alloantibodies crossing the placenta (only IgG, specifically subclasses IgG1 and IgG3, is actively transported) and destroying fetal/neonatal RBCs that carry the corresponding paternally-inherited antigen, producing extravascular hemolysis, anemia, and compensatory extramedullary hematopoiesis. ABO HDFN is a distinct, generally milder entity within this pathophysiology because the responsible antibodies — anti-A and anti-B — are naturally occurring rather than requiring a sensitizing exposure; this is why, unlike Rh HDFN, ABO HDFN can present in a first pregnancy, and why a negative maternal antibody screen (performed against reagent group O cells that lack A/B antigens) does not rule it out. Clinically and serologically, ABO HDFN tends to diverge from Rh HDFN in several source-grounded ways: onset of jaundice is early (12–48 hours) but generally mild, hemoglobin at birth is typically normal (contrasted with the anemia-at-birth picture of severe Rh HDFN), the DAT is often only weakly positive, and the peripheral smear shows spherocytes rather than the erythroblastosis characteristic of Rh HDFN. Management follows an escalating ladder — phototherapy at 460–490 nm to photoisomerize/conjugate bilirubin, IVIG to blunt Fc-receptor-mediated hemolysis if bilirubin trends upward, and double-volume exchange transfusion reserved for bilirubin unresponsive to these measures — because uncontrolled unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia risks kernicterus. Any cellular component transfused to a neonate under 4 months, including for exchange transfusion, must be irradiated (to prevent TA-GVHD) and CMV-reduced-risk, reflecting the general neonatal transfusion practice standards that apply regardless of the specific cause of HDFN.

**Pitfalls.**
- Assuming HDFN cannot occur in a first pregnancy — true for Rh HDFN (requires prior sensitization) but false for ABO HDFN, since anti-A and anti-B are naturally occurring antibodies not dependent on prior antigen exposure (SRC-017).
- Expecting a strongly positive DAT or anemia at birth in ABO HDFN — the DAT is often only weakly positive and hemoglobin is typically normal at birth in ABO HDFN, in contrast to the more severe, anemia-at-birth phenotype of Rh HDFN (SRC-017).
- Misreading a weak or missing ABO antigen reaction on a newborn's forward type as a true serologic discrepancy requiring extensive workup, rather than recognizing it as an expected, benign finding in neonates (SRC-024).
- Confusing spherocytosis (the smear finding typical of ABO HDFN) with erythroblastosis/nucleated RBC predominance (the smear finding typical of Rh HDFN) — these point to different diagnoses and different expected severities (SRC-017).

   - **Answer:** Emergent erythrocytapheresis (automated RBC exchange) targeting an HbS fraction below 30%
   - Red cell exchange is the apheresis procedure specifically used for acute complications of sickle cell disease; it rapidly reduces the HbS fraction below 30% for stroke prevention/treatment, a target and speed of correction a simple top-up transfusion cannot reliably achieve.
2. The blood bank must select red cell units for this large-volume exchange in a patient with SCD and a prior transfusion history. What selection approach should guide unit choice beyond a routine ABO/Rh crossmatch?
   - **Answer:** Select extended (phenotypic) antigen-matched units guided by molecular RBC genotyping
   - Molecular RBC genotyping is essential in sickle cell disease for providing extended antigen matching, since chronically/repeatedly transfused SCD patients are at high risk of alloimmunization; genotyping also identifies clinically important Rh variants that routine serologic typing cannot detect.
3. The molecular RBC genotype, sent as part of the SCD extended-matching workup, shows that her RHD gene encodes a partial D (DVI-type) hybrid allele, even though routine serology reported her as D-positive. How should this change management of both her red cell transfusion support and her obstetric Rh care?
   - **Answer:** She should be considered Rh-negative for transfusion purposes and for RhIg candidacy, to prevent sensitization to the D epitopes missing on her partial D cells
   - Partial D arises from RHCE/RHD hybrid genes producing a qualitative change in the D protein, with some D epitopes absent; patients with partial D can form allo-anti-D if exposed to conventional D-positive cells. Recipients with partial D (e.g., DVI) should therefore be considered Rh-negative for both transfusion and RhIg candidacy, unlike weak D (a quantitative reduction in D-antigen sites), where the individual is unlikely to make anti-D if transfused with D-positive blood.

**Key pathway.** Acute neurologic/vaso-occlusive complication of sickle cell disease (HbSS) in pregnancy → emergent erythrocytapheresis (automated RBC exchange), not simple transfusion, targeting an HbS fraction below 30% → red cell units selected via molecular-genotyping-guided extended antigen matching rather than routine ABO/Rh crossmatch alone, because chronically transfused SCD patients are at high alloimmunization risk → molecular RHD genotyping (sent as part of that extended-matching workup) unmasks a partial D (DVI-type) variant despite a routine D-positive serologic type → patient is managed as Rh-negative for both future red cell transfusion and RhIg candidacy to prevent sensitization to her missing D epitopes, in contrast to weak D (quantitative reduction in D sites), which does not carry this risk.

**Teaching.** Sickle cell disease produces acute vaso-occlusive and neurologic complications, such as stroke, that the transfusion service manages with red cell exchange rather than simple transfusion, because erythrocytapheresis can rapidly and controllably lower the HbS fraction below the 30% threshold used for stroke prevention/treatment. Because SCD patients typically require ongoing transfusion support and carry an elevated risk of red cell alloimmunization, molecular RBC genotyping is used to provide extended antigen matching beyond routine serology. That same molecular testing also serves a second, safety-critical role in these patients: unmasking RHD variants, such as partial D, that type as conventional D-positive on routine serology but carry epitope differences capable of provoking allo-anti-D. Recognizing partial D and distinguishing it from weak D changes both transfusion selection and RhIg candidacy — partial D patients are managed as Rh-negative, while weak D patients generally are not, because only partial D carries meaningful risk of anti-D sensitization.

**Pitfalls.**
- Treating a D-positive serologic result as sufficient for Rh management in a chronically transfused SCD patient: some patients with partial D type as conventional D-positive by routine serology yet can still form allo-anti-D and must be managed as Rh-negative for transfusion and RhIg purposes.
- Confusing partial D with weak D: weak D is a quantitative reduction in D-antigen sites and these individuals are unlikely to make anti-D if given D-positive blood, whereas partial D is a qualitative loss of D epitopes and carries real anti-D risk — the two are not managed interchangeably.
- Reaching for simple top-up transfusion instead of erythrocytapheresis when a rapid, controlled reduction in HbS fraction (target below 30%) is needed for an acute SCD complication such as stroke.
- Relying on routine ABO/Rh crossmatch alone for a chronically transfused SCD patient instead of molecular-genotyping-guided extended antigen matching, which both reduces alloimmunization risk and detects clinically significant Rh variants.

Given the retinal hemorrhages, headache, dizziness, and confusion in a patient with known elevated monoclonal IgM, plus a normal platelet count, normal peripheral smear (no schistocytes), and normal ADAMTS13 activity, what is the diagnosis — and why is this NOT TTP despite both conditions being treated with TPE?
   - **Answer:** IgM-driven hyperviscosity syndrome from Waldenström macroglobulinemia. It is not TTP because the patient lacks the defining TTP pentad findings (no thrombocytopenia, no microangiopathic hemolysis on smear, and ADAMTS13 activity is normal rather than severely deficient).
   - SRC-047 defines hyperviscosity syndrome as visual disturbances (retinal hemorrhages), headaches, and dizziness, common in Waldenström macroglobulinemia (IgM); TTP is defined by a pentad (MAHA, thrombocytopenia, neurologic signs, renal dysfunction, fever) with ADAMTS13 activity typically <10%, none of which this patient's normal platelets/smear/ADAMTS13 support.
2. Why is a single (or very few) session(s) of TPE expected to be highly effective for this patient's IgM-mediated hyperviscosity, whereas apheresis for an IgG-mediated antibody disease typically requires multiple sessions?
   - **Answer:** IgM is a large immunoglobulin that is predominantly intravascular (~80%), so a single TPE session accesses and removes most of the pathogenic IgM directly from the plasma being exchanged. IgG, in contrast, is about 60% extravascular, so tissue-stored IgG re-equilibrates into the vascular space between sessions, requiring repeated exchanges to progressively lower the total body IgG burden.
   - SRC-047 Common Board Traps and Distractors: 'IgM is primarily intravascular (80%), making TPE highly effective in a single session. IgG is 60% extravascular, necessitating multiple sessions to allow for equilibration.'
3. What plasma-volume (PV) exchange target should the initial TPE session use, roughly how much of the intravascular IgM will that remove, and should the team plan to escalate directly to a 2.0-PV exchange if symptoms persist after the first session?
   - **Answer:** A standard 1.0-PV exchange, which removes approximately 63%-70% of an intravascular substance like IgM. Escalating immediately to 2.0 PV is not the default next step — doubling the exchanged volume only adds about 20% more removal while doubling procedure time, cost, and risk, so additional 1.0-PV sessions with clinical reassessment are generally preferred over jumping to 2.0 PV.
   - SRC-047 Core Concepts: 'a 1.0-plasma-volume (PV) exchange removes approximately 63%-70% of an intravascular substance.' Common Board Traps: 'Increasing PV from 1.0 to 2.0 only increases substance removal by an additional ~20% while doubling the time, cost, and risk.'
4. What replacement fluid should be used for this TPE procedure, and why is FFP not the preferred choice here even though FFP (unlike albumin) actually contains immunoglobulins?
   - **Answer:** 5% human serum albumin is the standard replacement fluid for this indication, selected to minimize allergic/anaphylactoid reactions and disease-transmission risk. FFP is reserved for indications such as TTP, where it is needed to supply ADAMTS13 — despite FFP containing immunoglobulins and coagulation factors that albumin lacks, its higher risk of allergic reaction and citrate load makes it a poor default for routine TPE such as this hyperviscosity case.
   - SRC-047 Management: 'Most Other TPE: 5% Human Serum Albumin is the standard replacement fluid to minimize allergic reactions and disease transmission risk' versus 'TTP: Emergent TPE with Plasma (FFP) as the replacement fluid to provide ADAMTS13.' High-Yield Comparison: 'Plasma contains all factors but carries high risk of allergic reactions and citrate load; Albumin has low reaction risk but lacks coagulation factors and immunoglobulins.'

**Key pathway.** Diagnose IgM-driven hyperviscosity syndrome from underlying Waldenström macroglobulinemia (not TTP — platelets, smear, and ADAMTS13 are normal, so the TTP pentad is not met). Because IgM is ~80% intravascular, emergent therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) at a 1.0 plasma-volume (PV) exchange is expected to be highly effective after a single session (removing ~63-70% of intravascular IgM), unlike IgG-mediated antibody disease, which is only 60% extravascular-corrected (i.e., IgG is ~60% extravascular) and needs repeated sessions for equilibration. Escalating to 2.0 PV is not first-line, since it only adds ~20% further removal for double the time/cost/risk. Replacement fluid should be 5% albumin (not FFP) to minimize allergic reaction and citrate/disease-transmission risk; FFP is reserved for ADAMTS13-deficient TTP, even though FFP (unlike albumin) does contain immunoglobulins and coagulation factors.

**Teaching.** Plasma is a complex fluid whose protein constituents include albumin (oncotic pressure), coagulation factors (hemostasis), and immunoglobulins (immunity) (SRC-031). Immunoglobulin classes are not interchangeable from a kinetics standpoint: IgM is a large, predominantly intravascular molecule (~80% intravascular), so pathologic IgM excess — as in Waldenström macroglobulinemia — produces hyperviscosity syndrome (visual disturbance/retinal hemorrhage, headache, dizziness) and responds dramatically to a single TPE session because most of the pathogenic burden sits in the compartment being exchanged. IgG, by contrast, is about 60% extravascular, so IgG-mediated antibody diseases treated with apheresis require multiple sessions to allow extravascular IgG to re-equilibrate into plasma before it, too, can be removed (SRC-047). TPE volume kinetics follow first-order removal: a 1.0-PV exchange removes ~63%-70% of an intravascular substance, and escalating to 2.0 PV yields only ~20% additional removal for double the time/cost/risk — a "diminishing returns" board trap (SRC-047). Finally, replacement-fluid selection in TPE reflects a genuine trade-off around immunoglobulin (and coagulation factor) content: plasma (FFP) contains immunoglobulins and clotting factors and is required when those factors must be replaced (e.g., ADAMTS13 in TTP), but it carries higher allergic-reaction, citrate-load, and disease-transmission risk; albumin lacks immunoglobulins and coagulation factors but is the default replacement fluid for most other TPE indications, including hyperviscosity syndrome, because it minimizes those risks (SRC-047).

**Pitfalls.**
- Reflexively choosing FFP as the TPE replacement fluid for any plasma-protein disorder — FFP is reserved for indications like TTP (to replace ADAMTS13), not for paraprotein-removal syndromes such as hyperviscosity, where albumin is standard.
- Assuming all immunoglobulin-mediated conditions respond identically to TPE — IgM-driven disease (e.g., Waldenström hyperviscosity) responds rapidly to a single exchange because IgM is largely intravascular (~80%), whereas IgG-mediated disease needs repeated sessions because ~60% of IgG is extravascular and re-equilibrates between treatments.
- Escalating the exchanged plasma volume from 1.0 PV to 2.0 PV expecting proportional benefit — this adds only ~20% more removal while doubling time, cost, and risk.
- Conflating hyperviscosity syndrome with TTP because both present with neurologic symptoms and are treated with TPE — a normal platelet count, absence of schistocytes/MAHA, and normal ADAMTS13 activity argue against TTP and instead point to a paraprotein-driven hyperviscosity state.

## C-016 — Plasma Product Selection in Therapeutic Plasma Exchange for TTP  `C`
*7. Plasma Components and Derivatives* · support: moderate

**Setup.** A 42-year-old woman is brought to the emergency department with two days of progressive fatigue, easy bruising, and new intermittent confusion. She denies recent diarrheal illness, new medications, or bleeding history. On exam she is mildly febrile, has scattered petechiae over the lower extremities, mild scleral icterus, and a fluctuating mental status without a clear focal neurologic deficit. There is no evidence of a surgical or obstetric bleeding source. Given the constellation of severe thrombocytopenia, laboratory evidence of hemolysis, and neurologic change, the treating team suspects thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) and requests emergent blood bank/apheresis medicine involvement to arrange therapeutic plasma exchange and guide plasma product selection.

**Findings.**
- Platelet count: 14 x10^3/microL (critical)
- Hemoglobin: 7.9 g/dL (abnormal)
- Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH): markedly elevated (abnormal)
- Haptoglobin: undetectable (abnormal)
- Peripheral blood smear: numerous schistocytes present (abnormal)
- ADAMTS13 activity/inhibitor assay: sent, pending; clinical suspicion high for autoantibody-mediated ADAMTS13 inhibition
- Mental status: waxing-and-waning confusion, no focal deficit (abnormal)
- Ionized calcium (intra-procedure, during TPE): decreased, with perioral tingling and positive Chvostek sign (critical)

**Decision points.**
1. Given severe thrombocytopenia with microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes, elevated LDH, undetectable haptoglobin) and new neurologic changes, concerning for TTP, what is the primary emergent treatment, and why does it work?
   - **Answer:** Emergent therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE).
   - The board curriculum source identifies TPE as a primary treatment for TTP specifically because the exchange process removes the circulating ADAMTS13 inhibitor (autoantibody) driving the microangiopathy (SRC-010, Apheresis domain, high-yield fact: 'TPE is a primary treatment for TTP by removing ADAMTS13 inhibitors').
2. TPE requires a replacement fluid to restore the volume removed during exchange. Which class of plasma product should be selected for a patient undergoing TPE for TTP, and how does this differ from a purely volume/oncotic replacement strategy?
   - **Answer:** Plasma should be used as the replacement fluid rather than albumin alone.
   - The apheresis domain of the curriculum explicitly flags 'replacement fluid clinical aspects (Albumin vs. Plasma)' as a core decision point in therapeutic apheresis (SRC-010) rather than treating replacement fluids as interchangeable. In an ADAMTS13-inhibitor-driven disease such as TTP, where the source-documented therapeutic mechanism of TPE is removal of the inhibitor, plasma is the physiologically indicated replacement product because it is the plasma-derived fluid that can also supply functional ADAMTS13, unlike a non-plasma colloid.
3. Midway through the TPE procedure, the patient develops perioral tingling, hand cramping, and a positive Chvostek sign. What is the most likely cause?
   - **Answer:** Citrate toxicity causing acute hypocalcemia.
   - SRC-010 lists citrate toxicity presenting as hypocalcemia as a common adverse effect of apheresis procedures (Apheresis domain, high-yield fact 43), reflecting the citrate anticoagulant used in the extracorporeal circuit binding ionized calcium.
4. How should this citrate-toxicity/hypocalcemia event be classified and handled within the facility's quality system, and does it trigger FDA Biological Product Deviation (BPD) reporting?
   - **Answer:** It is documented as an adverse reaction, one of the three Nonconforming Event (NCE) categories (error, accident, adverse reaction), and managed through the facility's quality system; it is not automatically a BPD unless it reflects a manufacturing/process deviation from cGMP affecting product safety, purity, or potency, in which case FDA reporting would be due within 45 calendar days.
   - SRC-009 defines a Nonconforming Event as a failure to meet requirements, classified as an error, accident, or adverse reaction, and separately defines a Biological Product Deviation as an event during manufacturing (collection to distribution) that deviates from cGMP or affects product safety, purity, or potency, reportable to the FDA within 45 calendar days. A recognized, appropriately monitored physiologic citrate reaction during TPE fits the 'adverse reaction' NCE category rather than a manufacturing BPD.

**Key pathway.** Suspected TTP (severe thrombocytopenia + microangiopathic hemolytic anemia + new neurologic change) is a hematologic emergency treated with immediate therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE), because TPE removes the circulating autoantibody that inhibits ADAMTS13. Because the disease mechanism is an ADAMTS13 inhibitor, the replacement fluid for the exchange must be plasma (a plasma product) rather than a non-plasma colloid such as albumin alone — replacement-fluid selection (albumin vs. plasma) is itself a core apheresis decision point, and plasma is the fluid that also supplies functional ADAMTS13. During the procedure, the citrate anticoagulant used in the apheresis circuit can cause acute hypocalcemia (citrate toxicity), which must be recognized and managed promptly. Afterward, the reaction is documented through the facility's quality system as an adverse reaction — one of the three Nonconforming Event categories (error, accident, adverse reaction) — which is a distinct pathway from a Biological Product Deviation (BPD) report to the FDA, the latter being reserved for a manufacturing/process deviation from cGMP that affects product safety, purity, or potency.

**Teaching.** This case links the pathophysiology-to-regulatory arc that the exam expects for plasma product clinical use in the apheresis setting. First, the diagnosis of TTP is tied directly to a specific mechanism (an ADAMTS13 inhibitor) and a specific, source-identified treatment (TPE) that works by removing that inhibitor. Second, the case tests whether the learner understands that plasma products are not interchangeable with other colloids in apheresis: the curriculum explicitly frames 'Albumin vs. Plasma' as a clinical decision in replacement-fluid selection, and in an ADAMTS13-inhibitor disease, plasma is the product that both allows removal of the inhibitor via exchange and can replete the deficient enzyme, making it the clinically indicated plasma product here. Third, the case tests recognition of the single most commonly cited apheresis adverse effect — citrate toxicity/hypocalcemia — which the source calls out explicitly. Finally, the case extends into the regulatory/quality domain to test whether the learner can correctly triage an adverse event: distinguishing a Nonconforming Event 'adverse reaction' (a recognized physiologic reaction managed under the facility's quality system) from a Biological Product Deviation (a true manufacturing/process failure reportable to FDA within 45 days). This integration of clinical apheresis medicine with the quality/regulatory framework mirrors how blueprint domains (Apheresis and Administration/Laboratory Management) are tested together on the BB/TM exam.

**Pitfalls.**
- Confusing simple plasma infusion with therapeutic plasma exchange: TTP responds to TPE specifically because the exchange process removes the ADAMTS13 inhibitor; the source ties the mechanism of benefit to removal, not merely to plasma administration (SRC-010).
- Assuming albumin is an adequate, interchangeable replacement fluid for any apheresis procedure: the curriculum specifically calls out 'Albumin vs. Plasma' as a distinct clinical decision, signaling that fluid choice is disease-dependent rather than uniform across all apheresis indications (SRC-010).
- Reflexively labeling every adverse reaction during an apheresis procedure as an FDA-reportable Biological Product Deviation: per SRC-009, a BPD specifically requires a deviation from cGMP during manufacturing that affects product safety, purity, or potency, whereas a recognized physiologic reaction (such as citrate-induced hypocalcemia) is captured as an 'adverse reaction' under the facility's Nonconforming Event system.
- Blurring the three distinct Nonconforming Event categories (error, accident, adverse reaction) or conflating an adverse reaction with a 'near-miss': SRC-009 defines these as separate constructs with separate handling implications.

## C-017 — Autologous HPC Transplant: Graft Handling, Blood Support, and Shipping Pitfalls  `AR`
*16. Hematopoietic Progenitor Cell (HPC) Transplantation* · support: high

**Setup.** A 56-year-old man with relapsed diffuse large B-cell lymphoma achieves a chemosensitive second complete remission after salvage chemotherapy and is referred for high-dose chemotherapy with autologous hematopoietic progenitor cell (HPC) rescue. He will serve as his own HPC donor: peripheral blood stem cells are mobilized and collected by apheresis, cryopreserved, and later reinfused after myeloablative conditioning. Across collection, graft processing, peri-transplant supportive transfusion, and eventual disposition of a residual frozen aliquot, the transplant physician and blood bank must correctly apply the rules that make autologous HSCT biologically and operationally distinct from allogeneic HSCT.

**Findings.**
- Diagnosis / indication for HSCT: Relapsed diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, chemosensitive, in second complete remission
- Donor-recipient relationship: Autologous — patient is both donor and recipient (normal)
- ABO/Rh type: O positive (identical for 'donor' and recipient, as expected in autologous HSCT) (normal)
- HLA typing: Not performed / not required for autologous HSCT (normal)
- Pre-apheresis hemoglobin: 11.8 g/dL (normal)
- HPC product cryopreservation method: DMSO-based cryoprotectant with controlled-rate freezing (normal)
- Graft processing prior to freezing: Not irradiated; not leukoreduced (normal)
- Day +5 post-conditioning CBC: Severe pancytopenia with platelet count meeting transfusion threshold (expected pre-engraftment aplasia) (abnormal)
- Platelet product ordered for Day +5 transfusion: Irradiated, CMV-reduced-risk (leukoreduced) apheresis platelets (normal)
- Infectious disease testing status of residual cryopreserved aliquot slated for shipment to an outside storage facility: Required before release, despite autologous status

**Decision points.**
1. Before autologous PBSC collection, the transplant coordinator asks whether formal ABO and HLA compatibility testing between 'donor' and 'recipient' is required, as it would be for an allogeneic transplant. What is the correct answer?
   - **Answer:** ABO/HLA compatibility testing is not required, because donor and recipient are the same person, eliminating any risk of GVHD, graft rejection, or hemolytic ABO incompatibility from the graft.
   - Autologous HSCT has the lowest risk of immunologic complications of any HSCT type because donor = recipient (SRC-035, Autologous vs. Allogeneic HSCT comparison). HLA compatibility is described as paramount specifically 'in allogeneic HSCT' for engraftment and GVHD prevention, and ABO incompatibility (major/minor/bidirectional) is by definition a phenomenon of antigenic mismatch between two different individuals — a scenario that cannot occur when donor and recipient are the same person. HPCs also do not express ABO antigens, so ABO status never affects engraftment regardless of transplant type (SRC-035).
2. The apheresis-collected PBSC product is being readied for cryopreservation. The processing lab asks whether the graft should be irradiated (as is routine for cellular blood components) and leukoreduced before freezing. What is the correct instruction?
   - **Answer:** Never irradiate the graft and do not routinely leukoreduce it; cryopreserve using a DMSO- or glycerol-based cryoprotectant with controlled-rate freezing.
   - SRC-035 lists this as an explicit board trap: 'NEVER Irradiate the Graft... the HPC product itself must never be irradiated, as this would destroy the stem cells' ability to proliferate,' and 'Leukoreduction of Graft: Similar to irradiation, the graft should not undergo routine bedside leukocyte reduction.' SRC-034 confirms cryopreservation uses agents such as DMSO or glycerol with controlled-rate freezing for long-term storage.
3. On Day +5 after myeloablative conditioning, the patient develops severe thrombocytopenia during the pre-engraftment aplastic phase and requires a platelet transfusion. What processing must this platelet product undergo, and does this contradict the Stage 2 instruction never to irradiate the graft?
   - **Answer:** The platelets must be irradiated to prevent transfusion-associated GVHD and should be CMV-reduced-risk; this does not contradict Stage 2 because the 'never irradiate' rule applies specifically to the autologous HPC graft, not to standard cellular blood components given to support the patient.
   - SRC-035 states that 'all standard cellular blood components (RBCs, platelets) must be irradiated or pathogen-reduced for life to inactivate contaminating T-cells' to prevent TA-GVHD, and that CMV-reduced-risk components (leukoreduced or CMV-seronegative) should be used for these immunocompromised patients. This is explicitly distinguished from the graft-specific rule ('NEVER Irradiate the Graft') listed among the same source's common board traps — the two rules govern different products (the graft vs. supportive transfusion components) and are not in conflict.
4. Months later, the treating facility wants to transfer the patient's residual cryopreserved autologous HPC aliquot to an outside long-term storage facility. Does this shipment require infectious disease testing of the product, even though it is purely autologous and intended only for the same patient's own future use?
   - **Answer:** Yes — infectious disease testing is required whenever an autologous product is shipped to another facility, even though it will only ever be used for that same patient.
   - SRC-013 lists this explicitly as a common board trap: 'Autologous Testing: Autologous units do require infectious disease testing if they are shipped to another facility.' This sits within the broader FDA HCT/P risk-based regulatory framework (21 CFR Part 1271) described in SRC-034, which governs donor eligibility and infectious disease documentation whenever cellular products move between establishments.

**Key pathway.** Because donor and recipient are the same individual in autologous HSCT, ABO/HLA compatibility testing is unnecessary and there is no risk of GVHD, passenger lymphocyte syndrome, pure red cell aplasia, or hemolytic ABO incompatibility arising from the graft. The HPC graft itself must never be irradiated or routinely leukoreduced (either would destroy or deplete the stem cells' proliferative capacity), and is preserved long-term via DMSO- or glycerol-based cryopreservation with controlled-rate freezing. This "never irradiate/leukoreduce" rule applies only to the graft: once myeloablative conditioning produces pancytopenia, any standard cellular blood components (RBCs, platelets) transfused to support the patient must still be irradiated for life to prevent transfusion-associated GVHD, and should be CMV-reduced-risk, exactly as for any profoundly immunocompromised transplant recipient. Finally, autologous status does not exempt the cryopreserved HPC product from infectious disease testing if it is later shipped to another facility — testing is mandatory in that circumstance even though the product is for the patient's own future use.

**Teaching.** Autologous HSCT biology is defined by a single fact with cascading consequences: the donor and the recipient are the same person. This eliminates the antigenic mismatch that drives essentially every allogeneic-specific complication — GVHD, graft rejection, passenger lymphocyte syndrome, pure red cell aplasia, and major/minor/bidirectional ABO incompatibility all require a donor immunologically distinct from the recipient, so none of them occur in true autologous HSCT, and routine pre-collection ABO/HLA compatibility testing is unnecessary. Graft handling rules, however, are shared with allogeneic HSCT and are frequently confused with ordinary blood-component processing: the HPC product itself must never be irradiated (this destroys stem cell proliferative capacity) and should not undergo routine leukoreduction; it is instead preserved with a DMSO- or glycerol-based cryoprotectant using controlled-rate freezing. This graft-specific prohibition on irradiation is easily and dangerously confused with the opposite rule that governs supportive blood transfusions given to the patient during the pre-engraftment aplastic phase: because conditioning renders the patient profoundly immunosuppressed, every standard cellular blood component (RBCs, platelets) transfused to support them — not the graft — must be irradiated for life to prevent transfusion-associated GVHD, and CMV-reduced-risk components should be used. Finally, the regulatory dimension of autologous products is a distinct and separately tested point: even though a cryopreserved autologous HPC aliquot poses no compatibility risk to anyone but the original patient, shipping it to another facility still triggers a mandatory infectious disease testing requirement, reflecting the FDA's establishment-to-establishment HCT/P oversight framework rather than any immunologic concern.

**Pitfalls.**
- Assuming autologous HSCT requires ABO/HLA compatibility testing or matching as allogeneic HSCT does — it does not, since donor and recipient are the same individual.
- Irradiating or routinely leukoreducing the autologous HPC graft itself, confusing graft-processing rules with standard cellular blood component processing — this destroys stem cell viability and engraftment capacity.
- Assuming the 'never irradiate the graft' rule extends to blood components transfused to the patient for supportive care — it does not; those components must still be irradiated for life to prevent TA-GVHD.
- Attributing GVHD, passenger lymphocyte syndrome, or pure red cell aplasia to autologous HSCT — these are allogeneic-specific complications requiring donor-recipient antigenic disparity.
- Assuming autologous status exempts an HPC product from all infectious disease testing — testing is still mandatory if the product is shipped to another facility.

## C-018 — Transfusion-Transmitted Hepatitis: Donor Screening, Window Periods, and Mandatory Look-Back  `C`
*8. Infectious Hazards of Transfusion* · support: high

**Setup.** Two linked vignettes at a hospital-based blood center illustrate the donor-testing and recipient sides of transfusion-transmitted hepatitis. (1) A 52-year-old man presents as a first-time allogeneic whole blood donor. History is negative for jaundice, prior transfusion, or injection drug use, but he reports a Hepatitis B vaccine booster 10 days before donation for an upcoming work assignment. His unit is collected and quarantined pending mandatory infectious disease testing, consistent with the five-layer blood safety model (donor screening, deferral registry check, blood testing, quarantine, and investigation of manufacturing deficiencies). (2) Ten weeks after receiving an RBC transfusion at the same hospital, a 61-year-old man returns with new-onset jaundice, dark urine, and malaise. The treating team requests a transfusion-medicine consult, and the blood center is asked to review the donor record for the implicated unit.

**Findings.**
- Donor pre-donation history: No jaundice, prior transfusion, or IV drug use; Hepatitis B vaccine booster 10 days before donation
- Donor HBsAg immunoassay (index donation): Reactive (abnormal)
- Donor HBV NAT (individual-donation): Non-reactive (normal)
- Donor anti-HBc: Non-reactive (normal)
- Donor HBsAg supplemental neutralization test: Negative — nonspecific/vaccine-related reactivity, true infection not confirmed (normal)
- Recipient presentation, 10 weeks post-transfusion: New jaundice, dark urine, malaise (abnormal)
- Recipient anti-HCV: Reactive (abnormal)
- Recipient HCV RNA (NAT): Reactive (critical)
- Implicated donor's subsequent interval donation — anti-HCV and HCV RNA: Both reactive (critical)
- Regulatory retrieval/quarantine window after a reactive HIV, HCV, WNV, ZIKV, or T. cruzi test: Must be initiated within 3 calendar days

**Decision points.**
1. The donor's HBsAg immunoassay is reactive, but HBV NAT and anti-HBc are both non-reactive, and he received an HBV vaccine booster 10 days before donation. Is this reactive HBsAg proof of true acute HBV infection, and what should the blood center do before making a deferral decision?
   - **Answer:** Not necessarily true infection — perform HBsAg supplemental neutralization testing before final interpretation; the pattern is most consistent with transient vaccine-induced HBsAg positivity rather than confirmed HBV infection.
   - Donors may test transiently HBsAg-positive for up to 28 days after HBV vaccination, and repeatedly reactive serologic screens require supplemental testing (e.g., neutralization for HBsAg, Western blot for HIV) to differentiate true infection from a false-positive/nonspecific reactive screen before a permanent deferral decision is finalized.
2. The recipient's anti-HCV and HCV RNA both return reactive, and record review shows the implicated donor's next interval donation is also reactive for anti-HCV/HCV RNA. Which virus is most likely responsible for this posttransfusion hepatitis picture, and what mandatory regulatory process must the blood center now execute?
   - **Answer:** HCV — the most common cause of chronic posttransfusion hepatitis. Because the donor subsequently tested reactive for HCV, the blood center must execute the mandatory look-back process: retrieval/quarantine of components from prior donations, initiated within 3 calendar days of the reactive result, plus legally required recipient notification.
   - HCV is identified as the most common cause of chronic posttransfusion hepatitis, presenting with jaundice, dark urine, and malaise. Look-back is the mandatory process of identifying and notifying recipients of components from a donor subsequently found reactive for HIV or HCV specifically (21 CFR 610.46/47); retrieval and quarantine must begin within 3 calendar days of the reactive test, and recipient notification is legally required only for HIV and HCV, whereas notification for other relevant transfusion-transmitted infections is merely recommended by AABB/FDA guidance.
3. A fellow asks why residual transfusion-transmitted HBV risk remains proportionally greater than HIV or HCV risk despite universal NAT screening. How should this be explained in terms of window periods?
   - **Answer:** HBV has a substantially longer NAT window period (~18–26 days) than HIV (~9 days after NAT, down from ~22 days pre-NAT) or HCV (~7 days after NAT, down from ~82 days pre-NAT). A longer silent window means an infected donor is more likely to donate before the virus becomes detectable, so HBV contributes disproportionately to residual risk despite the same layered testing algorithm.
   - The source explicitly warns not to confuse window period lengths: HBV has a significantly longer NAT window period (~18–26 days) compared to HIV and HCV, and quantifies NAT's effect on shortening the HIV window (~22 to ~9 days) and HCV window (~82 to ~7 days). Residual risk is defined as the risk remaining despite all safety layers, driven primarily by window-period donations.
4. The donor whose interval donation confirmed HCV reactivity asks whether he can ever donate blood again. What is the correct answer regarding donor reentry?
   - **Answer:** He is not necessarily permanently barred — deferred donors may be reentered after a specific waiting period (e.g., 6 months for HCV) provided they have negative results on an FDA-defined reentry testing algorithm.
   - Donor reentry after deferral is permitted following a defined waiting period (the source gives 6 months for HCV as an example) and negative results on an FDA-defined testing algorithm confirming the donor is no longer reactive.

**Key pathway.** Both threads test the same testing/interpretation framework for transfusion-transmitted hepatitis. Donor A's isolated reactive HBsAg with non-reactive HBV NAT/anti-HBc, in the setting of a recent HBV vaccine, is NOT proof of infection — supplemental (neutralization) testing must be performed before deferral, because vaccine recipients can be transiently HBsAg-reactive for up to 28 days. The recipient's new jaundice/dark urine/malaise with reactive anti-HCV and HCV RNA, tied to a donor who later tests reactive on an interval donation, represents HCV — the most common cause of chronic posttransfusion hepatitis — and legally triggers the mandatory look-back pathway (retrieval/quarantine within 3 calendar days; recipient notification is legally required only for HIV and HCV, unlike other relevant transfusion-transmitted infections). The comparatively long HBV NAT window period (~18–26 days) versus HIV (~9 days post-NAT) and HCV (~7 days post-NAT) explains why HBV contributes disproportionately to residual transfusion risk despite universal NAT. The HCV-reactive donor may only return to donate after a defined deferral period (e.g., 6 months) and a negative FDA-defined reentry testing algorithm.

**Teaching.** This case links the two clinical faces of transfusion-transmitted hepatitis: the donor-side screening/interpretation problem and the recipient-side clinical/regulatory response. On the donor side, mandatory allogeneic testing includes HBsAg, anti-HBc, and HBV DNA (NAT) alongside anti-HCV and HCV RNA; a single reactive screening immunoassay is not equivalent to confirmed infection and must be resolved with supplemental testing (e.g., HBsAg neutralization) — a step made especially important here because recent HBV vaccination can cause transient HBsAg reactivity for up to 28 days, a classic source of false alarm. On the recipient side, posttransfusion hepatitis classically presents with jaundice, dark urine, and malaise, and HCV is specifically called out as the most common cause of chronic posttransfusion hepatitis. When a transfusion-implicated donor is subsequently found reactive for HIV or HCV markers, federal regulation (21 CFR 610.46/47) mandates the look-back process: retrieval/quarantine of components from that donor's prior donations within 3 calendar days, and legally required notification of recipients — a legal mandate that applies only to HIV and HCV, not to the broader list of relevant transfusion-transmitted infections (WNV, T. cruzi, Babesia, syphilis, etc.), where notification is only recommended. Underlying all of this is the concept of the window period and how NAT narrows it: HBV's NAT window period (~18–26 days) is meaningfully longer than HIV's (~9 days) or HCV's (~7 days), which is why HBV remains a disproportionate contributor to residual transfusion risk even under a fully layered, NAT-based safety system. Finally, deferral is not always permanent — donors may reenter the donor pool after a defined waiting period and a negative FDA-defined testing algorithm.

**Pitfalls.**
- Treating a single reactive HBsAg (or other) immunoassay as confirmed infection without supplemental/neutralization testing — recent HBV vaccination can cause transient HBsAg positivity for up to 28 days, mimicking a true reactive result.
- Assuming look-back notification is mandatory for every relevant transfusion-transmitted infection — it is legally required only for HIV and HCV (21 CFR 610.46/47); notification for other agents (WNV, T. cruzi, Babesia, syphilis) is recommended, not mandated.
- Reversing the window-period hierarchy — HBV's NAT window period (~18–26 days) is longer than HIV's (~9 days) or HCV's (~7 days) post-NAT, making HBV the greater residual-risk contributor despite universal NAT, not the lesser one.
- Conflating hepatitis viral risk with the single greatest current infectious hazard of transfusion overall — bacterial contamination (especially of room-temperature-stored platelets) is currently the most significant infectious risk to the blood supply, a distinct fact from hepatitis-specific window-period/look-back reasoning tested here.

## C-019 — Massive Transfusion in Hemorrhagic Shock: From PBM-Guided Resuscitation to TRALI Recognition  `C`
*10. Surgery Patients* · support: moderate

**Setup.** A 28-year-old man is brought to the trauma bay after a high-speed motor vehicle collision with a suspected splenic laceration. On arrival he is anxious, tachycardic, hypotensive, and has cool, clammy skin consistent with hemorrhagic shock. The trauma team requests urgent blood product support while surgical control of bleeding is arranged. Over the next several hours he receives multiple rapidly infused units of red cells, plasma, and platelets as part of resuscitation, and the transfusion service is asked to guide component therapy and to evaluate two new clinical changes that arise during the resuscitation.

**Findings.**
- Stage 1 - Blood pressure at presentation: 78/50 mmHg (critical)
- Stage 1 - Heart rate at presentation: 134 bpm (abnormal)
- Stage 1 - Skin/perfusion: Cool, clammy extremities, delayed capillary refill (abnormal)
- Stage 1 - Mental status: Anxious, mildly confused (abnormal)
- Stage 1 - Ordering pattern requested by surgical team: Standing "type and crossmatch 2 units" blanket order (abnormal)
- Stage 2 - New symptom after rapid multi-unit infusion: Perioral paresthesias, tingling in fingertips, shivering (abnormal)
- Stage 2 - Ionized calcium: Decreased (transient hypocalcemia) (abnormal)
- Stage 2 - Blood pressure during citrate symptoms: Mild further dip, improves with slowed infusion + calcium (abnormal)
- Stage 3 - Oxygen saturation, room air: 86% (<90%) (critical)
- Stage 3 - PaO2/FiO2 ratio: ≤300 mmHg (meets hypoxemia threshold) (abnormal)
- Stage 3 - Temperature: 38.6°C (new fever) (abnormal)
- Stage 3 - Blood pressure during pulmonary event: 84/54 mmHg, unchanged/low (critical)
- Stage 3 - Response to furosemide trial: No improvement (abnormal)
- Stage 3 - Chest X-ray: New bilateral infiltrates, no widened cardiothoracic ratio (abnormal)
- Stage 3 - Jugular venous distension / S3 gallop: Absent (normal)
- Stage 3 - BNP/NT-proBNP: Not elevated to ≥1.5× pre-transfusion baseline (normal)
- Stage 3 - Post-transfusion DAT: Negative (normal)
- Stage 3 - Plasma inspection for hemoglobinemia / haptoglobin / LDH: No hemoglobinemia; haptoglobin and LDH unremarkable (normal)

**Decision points.**
1. The patient is in hemorrhagic shock (BP 78/50, HR 134) with ongoing surgical bleeding. The surgical team's habit is to place a standing "type and crossmatch 2 units" order and reorder as needed. Should the transfusion service follow this blanket-order pattern, or use a different strategy to guide component support?
   - **Answer:** Move away from the blanket 2-unit ordering pattern and use a goal-directed, TEG/TEM-guided massive transfusion strategy under PBM/Transfusion Safety Officer oversight.
   - The regulatory/operations source states that Patient Blood Management, overseen by a Transfusion Safety Officer, uses data to improve outcomes, 'reduces the use of blanket orders for 2 units and utilizes TEG/TEM to guide therapy, preserving the supply and reducing costs.' In active hemorrhagic shock, reflexive fixed-unit ordering is a recognized inefficiency that PBM programs specifically target; viscoelastic testing allows component selection matched to the patient's actual coagulopathy rather than an arbitrary fixed quantity.
2. After several rapidly infused units of red cells and plasma, the patient develops perioral tingling, tingling in his fingertips, and shivering, with a further dip in blood pressure. What is the most likely explanation, and how should it be managed?
   - **Answer:** Citrate toxicity causing transient hypocalcemia from rapid, large-volume infusion of citrate-containing blood components; manage by slowing the infusion rate and providing calcium supplementation (oral or IV).
   - The apheresis sources describe citrate toxicity as presenting with 'perioral paresthesias, tingling in fingertips, shivering, and potentially tetany or arrhythmias,' arising because citrate chelates ionized calcium ('transient hypocalcemia, low ionized calcium'); management is 'slowing the infusion rate and providing calcium supplementation (oral or IV).' This citrate/calcium physiology is described in the source specifically for apheresis procedures using citrate anticoagulant, but the same chelation mechanism applies whenever citrate-anticoagulated components are infused rapidly in large volume, as in this massive transfusion setting - this is the only sourced description of the citrate-toxicity presentation and treatment available for this case, so confidence in this specific extrapolation is moderate rather than high.
3. Two hours later the patient develops sudden dyspnea, hypoxemia (SpO2 86% on room air), fever, and new bilateral infiltrates on chest x-ray; blood pressure remains low at 84/54 mmHg and does not improve after a furosemide trial. There is no JVD or S3, and BNP is not elevated ≥1.5× baseline. The post-transfusion DAT is negative with no hemoglobinemia and normal haptoglobin/LDH. What is the diagnosis, and how should it be managed?
   - **Answer:** TRALI - manage with aggressive respiratory support (often mechanical ventilation) and avoid diuretics, since TRALI is a noncardiogenic process.
   - TRALI is defined as new acute lung injury with bilateral infiltrates within 6 hours of transfusion, presenting with sudden respiratory distress, hypoxemia (O2 sat <90%), fever, and hypotension, with PaO2/FiO2 ≤300 mmHg. The source explicitly distinguishes TRALI from TACO by blood pressure (TACO = high, TRALI = low/normal) and diuretic response (TACO responds, TRALI does not) - both point away from TACO here (low BP, no JVD/S3, no BNP rise, no improvement with furosemide). AHTR is excluded by the negative DAT, absence of hemoglobinemia, and normal haptoglobin/LDH, which are the source's listed evidence for AHTR. Management of TRALI is aggressive respiratory support; diuretics should be avoided because TRALI is noncardiogenic, and using them (as would be appropriate for TACO) would not help and reflects a classic board trap.

**Teaching.** Transfusion support of a trauma patient in hemorrhagic shock is not just a matter of issuing units quickly - it requires a systems-based, goal-directed approach and vigilance for complications that can themselves mimic or worsen shock. First, operationally, Patient Blood Management programs (overseen by a Transfusion Safety Officer) exist specifically to move practice away from reflexive 'blanket' 2-unit ordering toward TEG/TEM-guided, goal-directed component therapy, which both improves care and preserves the blood supply. Second, during rapid multi-unit resuscitation, clinicians must recognize that citrate anticoagulant in blood components can chelate ionized calcium and produce a distinct, reversible syndrome (perioral/fingertip paresthesias, shivering, hypotension) that is corrected by slowing the infusion and repleting calcium - not by stopping the transfusion outright or treating it as an allergic reaction. Third, when respiratory and hemodynamic decompensation occurs during transfusion, the differential of AHTR, TRALI, and TACO must be worked through systematically: AHTR is suggested by hemoglobinuria, decreased haptoglobin, elevated LDH/bilirubin, and a positive DAT; TACO is suggested by hypertension, JVD, S3 gallop, an elevated BNP, and improvement with diuretics; TRALI is suggested by hypotension or normal blood pressure, lack of diuretic response, fever, and noncardiogenic bilateral infiltrates with hypoxemia. These entities require different, sometimes opposite, management (e.g., diuretics help TACO but do not help TRALI, which is noncardiogenic), so an incorrect diagnosis can lead directly to harmful treatment.

**Pitfalls.**
- Reflexively giving diuretics for any post-transfusion hypotension-plus-respiratory-distress picture: diuretics help TACO but do not help TRALI, which is a noncardiogenic process - the source explicitly flags blood pressure and diuretic response as the key distinguishing features between the two.
- Attributing perioral tingling, fingertip paresthesias, and shivering during rapid multi-unit transfusion to an allergic or febrile reaction rather than to citrate-induced hypocalcemia, leading to antihistamines/antipyretics instead of the correct treatment (slow the infusion, give calcium).
- Continuing a 'blanket' 2-unit crossmatch/reorder pattern in an actively hemorrhaging shock patient instead of activating a goal-directed, TEG/TEM-guided massive transfusion approach under PBM oversight, which is a described inefficiency PBM programs are meant to correct.
- Resuming or continuing a transfusion after a reaction with fever, chills, or pain: per the source, restart after antihistamine treatment is permitted only for isolated urticarial reactions, not for reactions involving fever, hypotension, or respiratory symptoms such as those seen here.

## C-020 — Platelet Refractoriness and the Febrile Platelet Transfusion in Acute Leukemia  `C`
*12. Platelets* · support: high

**Setup.** A 58-year-old man with newly diagnosed acute myeloid leukemia is postinduction chemotherapy, day +10, in the expected period of marrow aplasia. He is admitted, afebrile until this morning, and now reports new gum bleeding and scattered petechiae on his lower legs. He has no major hemorrhage (no melena, hematuria, or hemodynamic instability). Physical exam reveals oral mucosal petechiae, a temperature of 38.9°C, and a palpable spleen edge 3 cm below the left costal margin (attributed to leukemic infiltration). He has received several platelet transfusions over the preceding two weeks with progressively smaller increments noted on daily counts.

**Findings.**
- Platelet count (pre-transfusion): 8,000/µL (critical)
- Temperature: 38.9°C (abnormal)
- Spleen exam: Palpable splenomegaly, 3 cm below costal margin (abnormal)
- Body surface area (BSA): 1.8 m² (normal)
- Apheresis platelet unit dose: 3.3 × 10^11 platelets (normal)
- Platelet count, 1 hour post-transfusion: 12,000/µL (abnormal)
- Corrected Count Increment (CCI), 1 hour: ≈2,180 (abnormal)
- Subsequent transfusion event: Temperature 39.8°C with rigors, ~30 minutes into a later apheresis platelet infusion (critical)

**Decision points.**
1. With a platelet count of 8,000/µL in this stable (no active major bleeding), febrile AML patient with mucosal petechiae, should a prophylactic platelet transfusion be given now, and at what threshold logic?
   - **Answer:** Transfuse one apheresis platelet unit now — the count is below the restrictive prophylactic threshold
   - A restrictive trigger of 10,000/µL is standard for prophylactic platelet transfusion in stable, non-bleeding patients; higher triggers (50,000/µL) are reserved for major surgery, not routine prophylaxis. Per patient blood management principles, a single unit is the default starting dose ('why give 2 when 1 will do'), followed by reassessment.
2. One hour after transfusing a single apheresis platelet unit (3.3 × 10^11 platelets), the count is 12,000/µL (pre-transfusion 8,000/µL, BSA 1.8 m²). Calculate the Corrected Count Increment (CCI) and interpret it.
   - **Answer:** CCI ≈ 2,180 — a markedly reduced increment consistent with a poor response
   - CCI = (Post-Tx count − Pre-Tx count) × BSA / platelets transfused = (12,000 − 8,000) × 1.8 / 3.3 ≈ 2,180. This is the standardized formula used to evaluate platelet transfusion efficacy. Platelet refractoriness is defined as an inadequate posttransfusion count increment, which this blunted CCI demonstrates.
3. Given the blunted CCI, and noting the patient's active fever and palpable splenomegaly at the time of the poor response, what is the most appropriate interpretation and next step?
   - **Answer:** Attribute the poor increment first to a likely nonimmune cause (fever/possible infection, splenomegaly) and address these; reserve HLA/HPA antibody workup for refractoriness that persists across multiple transfusions
   - Platelet refractoriness can be immune-mediated (HLA or HPA antibodies) or nonimmune-mediated (e.g., sepsis, fever, splenomegaly, DIC). This patient has two documented nonimmune drivers (fever, splenomegaly) present at the time of the poor response, so these should be evaluated and treated before escalating to HLA/HPA antibody testing, which is more appropriate for persistent refractoriness unexplained by clinical factors.
4. During a later apheresis platelet transfusion, the patient develops a temperature spike to 39.8°C with rigors about 30 minutes into the infusion. What is the priority action, and why does the platelet component itself warrant particular concern?
   - **Answer:** Stop the transfusion immediately, keep IV access open, notify the transfusion service, and evaluate for a possible septic transfusion reaction — platelet components are stored at room temperature (20–24°C) with agitation and carry the highest bacterial contamination risk among cellular components
   - Platelets are recognized as high-risk for bacterial contamination; mitigation strategies (initial volume diversion, skin disinfection, mandatory bacterial culture or NAT at the blood center) exist specifically because of this risk, which is compounded by room-temperature storage with agitation. A new fever with rigors during a platelet infusion should not be reflexively attributed to the patient's baseline leukemic fever without considering a septic transfusion reaction.

**Key pathway.** Correct pathway: (1) With a platelet count of 8,000/µL in a stable, non-actively-bleeding but febrile AML patient, transfuse one apheresis platelet unit now — this is below the 10,000/µL restrictive prophylactic threshold (the 50,000/µL threshold is reserved for major surgery, not stable prophylaxis). (2) One hour later, calculate the Corrected Count Increment (CCI) using (Post-Tx count − Pre-Tx count) × BSA / platelets transfused: (12,000−8,000) × 1.8 / 3.3 ≈ 2,180 — a markedly blunted increment that meets the definition of platelet refractoriness (inadequate posttransfusion count increment). (3) Because the patient has active fever and palpable splenomegaly at the time of poor response, attribute the refractoriness first to nonimmune causes (sepsis/fever, splenomegaly, or DIC) and correct these; reserve HLA/HPA antibody testing and HLA-matched/crossmatched platelet selection for refractoriness that persists across multiple transfusions after nonimmune causes are excluded or treated. (4) On a subsequent apheresis platelet transfusion, a new temperature spike with rigors during the infusion should raise concern for a septic (bacterial) transfusion reaction — not simply be dismissed as an expected febrile reaction — because platelet components are stored at room temperature (20–24°C) with agitation and carry the highest bacterial contamination risk among cellular blood components; the transfusion should be stopped immediately, IV access maintained, and the transfusion service notified.

**Teaching.** Platelet-mediated hemostasis depends on both quantity (count) and quality (function) — a concept this case operationalizes by first applying an evidence-based numeric threshold (10,000/µL restrictive trigger for stable, non-bleeding patients, versus 50,000/µL for major surgery) and then interrogating whether transfused platelets are actually working. The Corrected Count Increment (CCI) is the standardized tool for that assessment, converting the raw platelet increment into a value normalized for body surface area and dose transfused, and a markedly low CCI operationally defines refractoriness. Refractoriness itself is not monolithic: the source material splits it into immune-mediated causes (HLA or HPA antibodies, which drive the search for HLA-matched or crossmatch-compatible platelets) and nonimmune causes (sepsis, fever, splenomegaly, DIC), which are far more common in the acute inpatient setting and should be sought and corrected before escalating to antibody-directed platelet selection. Separately, because platelet components are the only routinely stored blood product kept at room temperature with continuous agitation, they carry the field's highest recognized risk of bacterial contamination, which is why manufacturing controls (initial volume diversion, skin disinfection, mandatory bacterial culture/NAT) exist and why a new febrile episode during a platelet infusion deserves a different index of suspicion than the same fever in a chronically neutropenic patient at baseline. Finally, platelet "quality" extends beyond the transfusion service into perioperative planning — antiplatelet agents such as P2Y12 inhibitors are discontinued before elective surgery specifically to optimize platelet function, reinforcing that count alone never tells the whole hemostatic story.

**Pitfalls.**
- Equating a low platelet count with bleeding risk in isolation — platelet hemostatic efficacy depends on both quantity and quality (function), so count alone is an incomplete predictor.
- Reflexively pursuing HLA/HPA antibody testing and HLA-matched platelets on the first poor increment, without first excluding common nonimmune causes of refractoriness (fever, sepsis, splenomegaly, DIC) that are far more prevalent in acutely ill inpatients.
- Applying the 50,000/µL platelet threshold (reserved for major surgery/active bleeding contexts) to a stable, non-bleeding prophylactic scenario where the 10,000/µL restrictive trigger is the correct standard.
- Attributing a new fever during or shortly after a platelet transfusion solely to the patient's baseline illness without considering a septic transfusion reaction — platelets carry the highest bacterial contamination risk among cellular components because of room-temperature, agitated storage.
